# "Electric Cars Suck in the Winter" — Living With an EV in the Cold (@AutoFocus, 2026-02-13)

## Summary
A first-person explainer, not a review: Marques pushes back on the common "EVs are bad in
winter, so I could never own one" claim, drawing on **three or four winters** living with
his **Gen-1 Rivian R1T** in New Jersey (temps down to single digits Fahrenheit). His hot
take: he **prefers** driving an EV to a gas car in winter, once you weigh the one big
downside against several under-discussed upsides. **The one real downside is
road-tripping:** cold cuts range (his R1T drops from ~285 mi to ~230–240, sometimes
~200) *and* slows charging (the battery must spend energy reaching its ideal temperature),
and those compound across multiple stops — which is why many owners keep one gas car for
trips. **But** he argues the daily-driver math is fine *if you can charge at home*: you
start every day full, so as long as it covers your commute, winter range loss barely
matters. Then the upsides people don't talk about: (1) **preheat/precondition anywhere**,
including inside a garage (unlike a gas car you can't safely idle indoors), and near-
instant cabin heat (~15–30 s vs. 3–5 min for a gas engine to warm up); (2) **superior
winter traction** — EV electronic traction control adjusts the motors hundreds–thousands
of times per second, plus snow/mud/sand modes, so an EV can hold or recover grip a gas
pickup can't (cites videos of Rivians pulling F-150s and semis out of snow); (3) newer
**Gen-2 Rivians add a heat pump** (slightly smaller frunk) for better cold efficiency and
faster warm-up — a real but not drastic improvement; (4) no cold-idle warm-up needed —
get in and go. Conclusion: with home charging to 100% nightly, a winter EV is "actually
better." Marques-solo.

## Key claims
- [2026-02-13] Hot take: after 3–4 winters living with his Gen-1 Rivian R1T in New
  Jersey, he **prefers** an EV to a gas car in winter, on balance. (Marques)
- [2026-02-13] **Range loss is real:** his R1T falls from ~285 mi to ~230–240 in winter
  driving the same way, and can "plummet" to ~200 or less in extreme cold; cold is the #2
  range factor after driving style/speed/aero. (Marques)
- [2026-02-13] **Charging is slower in the cold** because the pack wants to be at a
  target temperature and must spend extra energy getting there — so EVs are worst-case
  for winter road trips (slower charge + more stops compound). This is why "a lot of
  people have one gas car and one EV." (Marques)
- [2026-02-13] **Home charging neutralizes the daily downside:** charging to 80–100%
  nightly means you start every day full, so whether you have 300, 200, or 100 mi, if
  it covers the commute the winter loss barely affects day-to-day. The downside only
  bites on long drives (and is much worse for people without home/garage charging).
  (Marques)
- [2026-02-13] Upside — **preheat/precondition anywhere, anytime,** including inside a
  closed garage (unsafe with a gas car); and EV cabin heat is near-instant (~15–30s,
  often seconds) vs. 3–5 min for a gas engine to warm up. (Marques)
- [2026-02-13] Upside — **winter traction:** EV electronic traction control modulates the
  motors hundreds–thousands of times per second for far more precise control; with
  snow/mud/sand modes an EV can hold traction or recover where a gas pickup can't (cites
  Rivians pulling F-150s / semis out of snow). "Much better at being under control in
  horrible road conditions." (Marques)
- [2026-02-13] **Gen-2 Rivians add a heat pump** (making the frunk slightly smaller than
  his Gen-1) for better efficiency and faster warm-up in the cold — handles extreme cold
  a little better, though "not actually that drastic" vs. his Gen-1. (Marques)
- [2026-02-13] Upside — no cold-idle warm-up: unlike performance gas cars where you let
  fluids circulate first, you can get into an ice-cold EV and drive immediately.
  (Marques)
- [2026-02-13] Conclusion: if you have a place to charge to 100% every night, a winter EV
  is "actually better" — his genuine preference. (Marques)
