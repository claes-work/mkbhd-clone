# Waiting for the Verizon Galaxy Nexus (@mkbhd, 2011-12-06)

## Summary
A comedic rant about Verizon's silence on the Galaxy Nexus launch — the phone had been
announced two months earlier, the international version was already out, and Verizon had
said nothing about a US date. He plays a sketch ("hey, important guy with a clipboard, do
you think we should release the Galaxy Nexus today?"), then makes a serious argument
underneath the bit: **the complaint isn't the delay, it's the absence of communication**,
and the branding fight is a symptom of the carrier treating a Google flagship as a Verizon
one.

## Key claims
- [2011-12-06] **The complaint is precisely scoped** — a distinction he states explicitly:
  "I don't care if this device is released December 31st. But if Verizon doesn't actually
  tell anyone about it, this goes down as the worst product launch I have ever seen."
  Silence, not lateness, is the failure.
- [2011-12-06] **Why a Nexus is different**: "Nexus devices are always the reference device
  for what Google built this new OS on" — the reference-hardware argument he returns to
  throughout his Nexus coverage.
- [2011-12-06] **Branding-control critique (continues the carrier-control thread)**: "we've
  seen leaked shots of the Nexus that's supposed to come to Verizon and I can't even find any
  Google branding on it. This is supposed to be a Google flagship device, not necessarily a
  Verizon flagship." Same structural lens as the ad teardown three weeks earlier
  ([[2011-11-15-yt-sK_M5gRMP1I]]) — who controls the presentation, and what it costs.
- [2011-12-06] **Fair-minded concession before the criticism**: he grants the real engineering
  reasons for the US delay — thicker body, larger battery, LTE radio — "I get that. But
  Verizon, please don't get carried away." The complaint is narrowed rather than maximal.
- [2011-12-06] Dated launch context: Google presented the phone two months prior and promised
  "not to steal carrier thunder," implying a release by year's end; he names December 9th as
  the date he's hoping for and "the last straw."
- [2011-12-06] **Self-aware consumer honesty**: "I keep telling myself I don't need it. I
  don't need it. I don't—" — the anticipation is played as a personal weakness, not a
  reviewer's detachment.
