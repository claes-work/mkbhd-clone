# Talking Tech and The Metaverse with Mark Zuckerberg! (@mkbhd, 2020-09-16)

## Summary
A Marques Brownlee × Mark Zuckerberg "Talking Tech" interview, framed by Marques (in an editor's-cut intro) as a deliberately narrow 15-minute conversation about tech — he preempts comments asking why he didn't grill Zuckerberg on policy or the 2020 election, saying they'd slotted 15 minutes to "talk tech" and this is that. Marques drives the conversation as interviewer, steering it almost entirely to VR/AR, since Zuckerberg heads Oculus. Zuckerberg is the GUEST: he lays out his thesis that a new major computing platform arrives roughly every 15 years, that VR/AR is the next one, and that its essence is "presence" — the feeling of actually being with another person (spatial audio, passing objects, sub-10ms latency) rather than seeing them on a screen. He describes a 5–10-year future of sleek goggles that "transport" you into someone's studio, AR glasses that project holograms and could replace physical TVs/tablets, and new machine-provided senses (a proximity sense to objects). Marques connects this to his own craft — asking what the next version of tech reviews looks like a decade out, offering VR ("put you in my shoes") and asking if that's a dumb answer. The video ends with a "this or that" rapid-fire. Because this is an interview, only Marques-attributed material is persona/voice data; all of Zuckerberg's substantive answers are quarantined below and excluded from persona training.

- [2020-09-16] (Mark Zuckerberg — CONTEXT) Splits his time by picking a "theme for a week" (usually two or three things) to focus on, relying on strong leaders running each app to avoid constant context-switching.
- [2020-09-16] (Mark Zuckerberg — CONTEXT) Thesis: new major technology platforms arrive roughly every 15 years, each more immersive. Phones are powerful but "anything but immersive" and pull you away from the world; the next platform must deliver "presence" — the sense you're actually with another person, via spatial audio, being able to pass objects, and real-time rendering faster than the eye's ~5–10ms refresh.
- [2020-09-16] (Mark Zuckerberg — CONTEXT) VR/AR is the first platform that convinces "your system" you're in a different place — other platforms make you "convince your brain" you're elsewhere while you sit on the couch.
- [2020-09-16] (Mark Zuckerberg — CONTEXT) Agrees VR fits Marques's future-of-reviews idea; says Facebook is "probably investing the most in virtual and augmented reality of anyone in the world"; envisions 5–10 years out: sleek goggles that transport you into a creator's studio where you can be handed and play with the product; AR as the lighter-weight version (holograms in normal glasses); mature AR glasses could remove the need for physical TVs/tablets, turning them into holographic apps and unlocking creativity/innovation.
- [2020-09-16] (Mark Zuckerberg — CONTEXT) "The real thing will always have somewhat [more] fidelity"; haptics/weight are hard to model, but AR/VR could add new senses — e.g. feeling resistance as your hand nears an object (like animals navigating by sonar). It won't replace all physical things, but many media (books, games, TVs) may not need to be physical objects in the future.
- [2020-09-16] (Mark Zuckerberg — CONTEXT) On form factor: VR looks matter less (used at home) but he still wants it smaller/lighter/cooler; AR has a very high aesthetic bar — it must look like normal (thick-rimmed) glasses, and he doubts all the electronics fit in thin rims anytime soon. Quest 2 is ~10% lighter than Quest 1 (~60g off), smaller, with a soft strap replacing the hard strap, and dropped in price from $399 to $299.
- [2020-09-16] (Mark Zuckerberg — CONTEXT) On Google Glass: characterizes it as "a smart watch on your face" — notifications/info in the corner of your eye; some may like that, but it's not what he's excited to build. He comes at this from "how do we connect people better," so a hologram of a person you can interact with is a "completely different value proposition" worth waiting for.
- [2020-09-16] (Mark Zuckerberg — CONTEXT) Rapid-fire answers: he's "the algorithmic guy" (chronological feeds encourage spam and make people miss milestones; ranking forces good content, though a chronological option is a fine balance); favorite non-Facebook apps are PurpleAir (an air-quality app, because of the California wildfires) and Spotify, praising founder Daniel Ek; his phone is a Samsung/Android (he thinks it's good for him and the management team to use Android since most of Facebook's billions of users are on Android); and he's a self-described "Sweet Baby Ray's fanboy."
