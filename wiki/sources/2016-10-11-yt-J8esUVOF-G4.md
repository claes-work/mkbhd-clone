# Dope Tech: 8K RED Epic-W Unboxing! (@mkbhd, 2016-10-11)

## Summary
Marques unboxes the RED Epic-W, a brand-new addition to RED's lineup and the first public look at it, built around RED's new super 35 8K "helium" sensor. He situates it on RED's product timeline (RED alternates yearly between new bodies and new sensors), positions the Epic-W between the lower-end Raven/Scarlet-W and the top-end Weapon, and explains that the cameras at this level are "computers attached to sensors" — so the Epic-W shares the exact helium sensor (8K, dynamic range, REDCODE RAW, RED color science) with the Weapon but has different internals (maxes at 30fps 8K vs. the Weapon's 75fps, higher compression floor) and an aluminum-alloy body instead of the Weapon's carbon fiber. He notes the video itself is shot on his own freshly helium-upgraded carbon-fiber Weapon, walks through why you can't see the 8K difference on YouTube (compression, 4K publishing ceiling, immature 8K editing support), and lays out his core POV: you shoot 8K now for the same reason people shot 4K to publish 1080p — sharpness, crop/zoom latitude, and extra data for stabilization/tracking/graphics — all in service of "the most realistic looking videos possible."

## Key claims
- [2016-10-11] RED releases an upgrade roughly every year, alternating between a new body and a new sensor (new body, new sensor, new body, new sensor).
- [2016-10-11] The new helium sensor is a super 35 8K sensor and is, in his words, overkill for YouTube.
- [2016-10-11] The helium sensor ships in two RED bodies: the Weapon (which he covered in a prior video) and the brand-new Epic-W, shown publicly for the first time in this video.
- [2016-10-11] The Epic-W sits between the lower-end Raven and Scarlet-W and the highest-end Weapon, keeping most helium-Weapon benefits at a lower price.
- [2016-10-11] The Epic-W retains RED's modular system and full accessory compatibility with the Weapon and any other RED.
- [2016-10-11] The Epic-W uses the exact same helium sensor as any other helium camera: 8K resolution, the dynamic range, REDCODE RAW, and RED color science.
- [2016-10-11] Because these cameras are effectively computers attached to sensors, the Epic-W's different body means different internals and processing power — it maxes out at 30fps at 8K versus the Weapon's 75fps, and can't hit the Weapon's lowest compression ratios.
- [2016-10-11] The Epic-W body is an aluminum alloy (all metal) whereas the Weapon is carbon fiber, so the two have different weight — a significant draw for many buyers.
- [2016-10-11] This very video is shot on his own maxed-out carbon-fiber Weapon, freshly upgraded to the new 8K helium sensor.
- [2016-10-11] You can't perceive the 8K difference on YouTube because every clip looks ~10x better before upload compression, and because the video is published in 4K, not 8K.
- [2016-10-11] 8K workflows are still immature: most editors don't support 8K edits/exports, Final Cut Pro isn't fully ready, Adobe only has a Premiere beta to handle the footage, and YouTube largely won't publish 8K — 4K is the max resolution for an H.264 file — so the footage was shot in 6K, which Final Cut can handle, and exported to 4K.
- [2016-10-11] Helium is supposed to dramatically improve low-light performance (historically a RED weakness) despite its smaller pixels; he's already comfortable at ISO ~2000–4000 and higher where older RED wasn't.
- [2016-10-11] He won't publish in 8K for a long while (no one has 8K or even 4K displays), but shooting 8K now pays off the way shooting 4K did — extra sharpness, crop/zoom without losing detail, and more data points for motion tracking, graphics, and stabilization.
