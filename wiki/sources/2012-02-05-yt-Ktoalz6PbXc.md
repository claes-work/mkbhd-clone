# Too Many Android Phones? RANT (@mkbhd, 2012-02-05)

## Summary
**The most fully-argued essay in the 2011–2012 material** — a sustained response to the
complaint that Android phones ship too often, and to the claim that "there is no right time
to buy an Android phone." He does five things in sequence: grants the complaint's factual
basis with examples; argues that **obsolescence is psychological, not functional**; relocates
the real problem to developer fragmentation; explains why manufacturers will never slow down
(**"these companies live by the quarterly report"**); and closes with a **buying framework**
that answers the question people actually keep asking him.

## Key claims
- [2012-02-05] **The complaint is granted first, with specifics**: the Droid RAZR followed
  months later by the RAZR MAXX with a bigger battery; the Transformer Prime followed at CES by
  the TF700T with a better display and camera; "AT&T and Samsung just went completely insane"
  with a Galaxy S2, an S2 Skyrocket and an S2 Skyrocket HD "all within a couple of months."
- [2012-02-05] **Core argument — nothing actually becomes obsolete**: "your phone doesn't just
  stop working when a new phone comes out… your current phone loses no functionality. It's
  still the awesome phone it was when you bought it… everything that was good about the phone
  that made you buy it is still good about that phone." He explicitly appeals to the definition
  of *obsolete*, and calls words like "outdated" and "old" **"almost vague or meaningless"** as
  applied to a three-month-old phone.
- [2012-02-05] **The Ferrari analogy** carries the point: buying an F430 and then seeing an F430
  Scuderia go past doesn't change what you're driving — "but you're still driving a brand new
  Ferrari."
- [2012-02-05] **The real problem relocated to development and support**: differing internals,
  screen resolutions, OS versions and skins mean "app developers out there… are going to say,
  screw developing six different versions of my app just to work on the phones out there — I'll
  just develop one version of my app for the iPhone." Fragmentation is defined as a consequence
  of that, not as a slur.
- [2012-02-05] **First-generation buyers are beta testers**: "when you buy a first generation
  device you're really beta testing" — it will take updates, an OS upgrade and firmware fixes
  before settling into the stable device you bought.
- [2012-02-05] **The incentive explanation, stated bluntly** — the clearest version of this
  reasoning pattern so far: "these companies live by the quarterly report, and they are never
  going to slow down so much that they make less money than they do now, because in the end
  finance is the main goal of these manufacturers." He dismisses PR statements about slowing
  down ("some random marketing rep from HTC may say, oh yeah, we're going to slow down phones
  this year") as contradicted by the incentive.
- [2012-02-05] **Steel-mans the release treadmill — the counter-intuitive turn**: if manufacturers
  released one device a year you would *lose* things — no incremental fixes like the RAZR MAXX's
  bigger battery, which existed precisely because "Motorola listened to you guys"; and
  "companies would be less likely to take risks if they're putting all their eggs into one
  basket once a year," so you wouldn't get designs as bold as the RAZR at all.
- [2012-02-05] **Locates the fix on the consumer side**: "the adjustment isn't going to happen on
  the manufacturer side, it's going to have to come for us on the consumer side."
- [2012-02-05] **Names the emotional driver**, pointedly: complaining makes sense only "unless you
  have some sort of inferiority complex, or if you absolutely must have the best phone out to
  feel good about yourself as a person, or if your phone is some sort of status symbol."
- [2012-02-05] **The buying framework (his standing answer to "what Android phone should I
  get?")**: list the features you actually want → filter to phones matching that set → watch
  reviews, handle it in a store, borrow a friend's → pick your favourite from the shortlist.
  "Get the device that fits you best and you'll be happy with it, because you made that correct
  choice."
- [2012-02-05] **Personal position stated**: "I personally really like all the choices that I get
  with the multitude of Android devices available for me to pick from — but I know a lot of you
  guys feel differently."

## Notes
- **Attribution: solo.** Marques only.
- ★★ **L3-candidate (beliefs — highest priority in this batch).** This single video contains
  four separately promotable positions: (1) obsolescence is perceptual, not functional;
  (2) the real cost of device proliferation is developer fragmentation, not consumer harm;
  (3) manufacturers answer to quarterly earnings, so expecting restraint is naive; and (4) the
  feature-first buying framework. Each is dated, first-person and argued rather than asserted.
- ★★ L3-candidate (method): this is the **maturest form of the incentive-structure reasoning**
  tracked since 2011-11-15 — and here it is stated as a general law of the industry ("live by
  the quarterly report") rather than applied to a single case. It also demonstrates the
  **steel-man move**: he argues the *benefits* of the thing he's defending against, then shows
  what the critics' preferred world would cost them. That is a distinctly higher gear of
  argument than his earlier rants, and it arrives seven weeks after he described studying
  consumer behavior ([[2011-12-14-yt-hx0na_NEfTQ]]).
