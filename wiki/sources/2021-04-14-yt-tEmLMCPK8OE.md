# Retro Tech: Teleportation (@mkbhd, 2021-04-14)

## Summary
A Retro Tech (Season 2) documentary reframing teleportation as the ancestor of virtual reality — the idea of instantly putting your consciousness "somewhere else." Marques hosts, unboxing Nintendo's 1995 **Virtual Boy** as the retro artifact, and threads three ideas: (1) the sci-fi teleporter dream (Star Trek's transporter); (2) the physics of why teleporting matter is essentially impossible, explained via a **double-slit / quantum-entanglement** segment with **Kevin (Vsauce2)**; and (3) VR as the achievable "bring B to A" substitute, from Jaron Lanier's 1980s EyePhone through a nausea-inducing "Dope or Nope" retro-VR gauntlet with **Jake (Game Ranks)**, to Palmer Luckey's low-latency head-tracking breakthrough that revived VR. It culminates in a philosophical sit-down with astrophysicist **Dr. Neil deGrasse Tyson** on whether a reassembled you is still you (Tyson: "It is a copy of you"). Marques closes embracing the inversion — instead of moving yourself from A to B, "bringing B to A instantly." Professionally captioned with reliable speaker labels.

## Key claims
- [2021-04-14] Framing: today's VR is "the closest thing we have to teleportation" — the 1995 Virtual Boy is "the early version of that." (Marques, narration)
- [2021-04-14] Teleportation defined: instantaneous, "no line" travel — step into a device, rematerialize on a matching device elsewhere at the speed of light; iconic via Star Trek's transporter, which "drilled home that this could be future tech." (Sherri, Keith, Blake, interviewees)
- [2021-04-14] Physics (with Kevin/Vsauce2): teleporting is only possible for "really really small things, like a photon," via quantum entanglement. The **double-slit experiment** shows a light beam would scatter into an interference pattern ("a scattered mess ... bad for you"); atoms behave as both waves and particles, and merely observing atoms in motion changes their destination — "to this day scientists have still no clue why." (Kevin, interviewee)
- [2021-04-14] VR history: Jaron Lanier's 1980s **EyePhone** (E-Y-E) introduced VR-as-a-product to the masses; a giant headset needing a huge, expensive computer. '90s arcade VR (Dactyl Nightmare) looked futuristic but made people sick (motion sickness) — "none of them actually did much, but they looked cool when your friends came over." (Greg, Blake, interviewees)
- [2021-04-14] Dope or Nope verdicts (with Jake/Game Ranks) — all retro VR rigs get **nope**: Stuntmaster VR (1993, "worst Gameboy in my face"), SegaScope 3-D (flickering, nausea), VR Ninja (a metal-ball contact motion sensor, "not an accelerometer," stabbed his face). "For the first time we've given all nopes, and yet I still think ... they're on to something." (Marques & Jake)
- [2021-04-14] VR's real revival: ~2012, when VR was "a joke, a punch line," teenager **Palmer Luckey** used low-latency gyroscopes/accelerometers and head tracking to "trick your brain into thinking you're somewhere else" — "really bringing it back to teleportation." Analogy to the pre-2007 inconceivability of the iPhone: maybe teleportation is closer than we think. (Blake & commentators, interviewees)
- [2021-04-14] Neil deGrasse Tyson on the hard problem: teleportation is fundamentally "an information processing challenge" — you can convert matter to energy (done in particle accelerators) but you need "the instruction set to reassemble you," including "the neurosynaptic memories ... in your brain's molecules." A Star Trek reassembly "is a copy of you ... basically cloning"; and it must happen simultaneously to count as moving rather than copying. On VR sufficing: "if you could smell the air and feel the breeze and watch the birds fly over it, maybe that's sufficient ... at that point I don't think it matters whether you actually go to the Grand Canyon." (Dr. Neil deGrasse Tyson, interviewee)
- [2021-04-14] Marques's closing thesis: the useful switch is "instead of teleporting ourselves from point A to point B, the tech that ... makes a lot more sense is bringing B to A instantly ... that is now the new future that I'm really looking forward to." (Marques)
