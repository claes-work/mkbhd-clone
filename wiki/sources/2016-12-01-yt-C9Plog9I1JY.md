# Dope Tech: Self Lacing Nike HyperAdapt 1.0! (@mkbhd, 2016-12-01)

## Summary
A Dope Tech first-look at the Nike HyperAdapt 1.0, the first self-lacing shoe people can actually buy and wear, framed explicitly against the earlier Nike Mag (the Back to the Future prop that was a near-unattainable collectible you'd display in a glass case). Marques's through-line is that the HyperAdapt succeeds where the Mag couldn't: it looks like a normal, wearable shoe while adding the self-lacing feature, so it works as an everyday shoe rather than a showpiece. He walks through the mechanics (up button to tighten, down button to loosen), the side/back LEDs that double as a battery gauge (blue good, yellow getting low, red low), roughly two-week battery life because the lights don't stay on 24/7, a two-amp magnetic wireless charger that recharges in about two hours from zero, an adjustable tightness memory preset, and a sub-15-oz weight despite a motor, battery, magnets and LEDs in the sole. His core POV: treat these as a tech product, and they read as a version 1.0 — like a first-gen smartphone or tablet, the tech has a long way to go but it's a milestone worth hanging onto. He does not state a retail price. He closes by giving his pair away in a joint giveaway with John of TLD Today.

## Key claims
- [2016-12-01] The Nike Mag he previously covered was a prop — a super-limited collectible almost no one could get, and even owners would display it in a glass case rather than wear it daily.
- [2016-12-01] The HyperAdapt 1.0 is the real, buyable-and-wearable self-lacing shoe Nike said it would make, arriving in 2016.
- [2016-12-01] The reviewed pair is a custom all-white colorway not everyone can get; the version people can actually buy comes in black, looks more subtle in person, and Marques prefers the black.
- [2016-12-01] Unlike the Mag (which looked like Moon boots), the HyperAdapt does a good job looking like a normal shoe while adding the self-lacing feature.
- [2016-12-01] Tightness is adjusted with side buttons — up to tighten, down to loosen — the same control scheme as the Mag.
- [2016-12-01] The side and back LEDs serve as a battery gauge: blue is good to go, yellow means getting lower/consider charging, and red means low battery.
- [2016-12-01] Unlike the Mag, the HyperAdapt's lights don't stay on 24/7 while worn, which saves battery and yields roughly two weeks of life per charge (Nike reps claimed even longer).
- [2016-12-01] The shoes charge wirelessly via a double-stethoscope-looking two-amp charger using powerful magnets in both the charger and the sole, taking about two hours to fully recharge from zero.
- [2016-12-01] You can top them up quickly from a power bank in an emergency dead-battery situation — which Marques calls "a very 2016 sentence."
- [2016-12-01] The shoes have a memory: hold both buttons for a couple of seconds to save your preferred tightness as a preset the shoe remembers next time you step in.
- [2016-12-01] Despite housing a motor, battery, magnets and LEDs in the sole, the shoe is lightweight — under 15 oz — and comfortable.
- [2016-12-01] Framed as a tech product, the HyperAdapt is a version 1.0 (as its name implies) — comparable to a first-gen smartphone or tablet, with tech that still has a long way to go.
- [2016-12-01] Marques is giving his pair (size 13) away alongside John of TLD Today (size 11), each bundling a tech backpack, a battery bank, and the winner's choice of smartphone; entry is a comment on both channels, with a winner picked one week later and announced on Twitter.
