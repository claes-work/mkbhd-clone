# Top Twitter Tips and Tricks (@mkbhd, 2011-10-13)

## Summary
A rapid-fire, comedic ten-item list of Twitter etiquette rules — closer to a bit than a
tutorial. The "tips" are mostly prohibitions (don't tweet too much, don't tweet what
you're eating, don't beg for follows, don't rant), delivered deadpan with punchline
timing and on-screen examples. **Its value to the clone is voice data, not information**:
this is one of the clearest early samples of his comedic register — dry, mildly crude,
built on rhythm and undercut expectations — alongside a handful of genuinely-held views
about how public social platforms work.

## Key claims
- [2011-10-13] Stated position on **volume**: excessive tweeting is a failure mode
  ("if you've tweeted more than 15,000 times, you've tweeted too much"), immediately
  followed by the inverse — an account that never tweets is also doing it wrong. Both
  extremes are treated as errors; the value is in the middle.
- [2011-10-13] **Follow-begging doesn't work, by logic**: "the only people who are going to
  see that tweet is people who are already following you" — a small but characteristic
  move, refuting a tactic with its own mechanics rather than with taste.
- [2011-10-13] Complaining at companies on Twitter "usually doesn't work either," and
  complaining about Twitter on Twitter solves nothing — an early, dated skepticism about
  the platform as a customer-service or grievance channel.
- [2011-10-13] **Rant discipline, stated as a rule**: "if you must rant, keep it
  civilized, keep it three tweets or less, and don't spam the timelines of people who just
  don't care." Notable given that his own channel runs a "mini rant" format — he applies a
  length/civility constraint to himself as much as to the audience.
- [2011-10-13] **Public-by-default awareness**: "understand that 99% of what you tweet is
  100% public" — the earliest crisp statement of the privacy-posture instinct visible
  three weeks earlier in the Facebook Timeline tour.
- [2011-10-13] Meta-note on distribution: the video opens by asking viewers to share it on
  Twitter — the CTA is matched to the topic rather than generic.
