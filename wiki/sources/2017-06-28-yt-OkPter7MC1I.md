# Dope Tech: "Shot on Smartphones!" (@mkbhd, 2017-06-28)

## Summary
A solo Dope Tech episode where Marques pulls back the curtain on "Shot on iPhone"-style smartphone commercials and then surveys the affordable gear that brings a slice of that capability down to the consumer. His framing: those clips *are* technically shot on smartphones, but often with heavy rigging — phones on drones, on cine lenses with wireless follow-focus and gimbals, on motorized four-wheeled skateboards — so the ads meant to make you feel "you can make movies just like that" can have the opposite effect once you see the behind-the-scenes, because that stuff isn't available to "99% of us mere mortals." He then walks through accessible dope tech to maximize the phone camera: Moment lenses (V2) as case-mounted cine lenses (18mm wide, fisheye, 10× macro) that trade upgrade-flexibility for centered-on-sensor precision and no vignetting; the DJI Osmo Mobile as the smartphone-stabilization standard; and a heavier Zhiyun Crane gimbal (via a Joby GorillaPod adapter) when you add lens weight the Osmo can't carry. His recurring thesis: a dedicated camera will always win on quality, but the smartphone will always be smaller, cheaper, and at your side — and it's plenty to start with. He closes by telling aspiring creators flooding his inbox to just start their YouTube channel with the phone camera they already have.

## Key claims
- [2017-06-28] "Shot on smartphone" commercials are technically real — the footage is captured on phones — but often with heavy adaptation; a perfect handheld shot in perfect conditions is possible but rare, and better shots are near-guaranteed with a bit of gear.
- [2017-06-28] The impressive commercial shots frequently rely on elaborate rigs: phone on a selfie stick or mount (no attachment), but also phone on a tiny drone, on a much bigger drone, or attached to a cine lens with a wireless follow-focus on a gimbal mounted to a motorized remote-control four-wheeled skateboard.
- [2017-06-28] The intended effect of these ads is aspirational — "you can make movies just like that" because you own the same phone — but seeing the behind-the-scenes can invert it, since that production setup is out of reach for "99% of us mere mortals."
- [2017-06-28] Smartphone cameras are, for the most part, really good and are now the most popular camera in the world; he endorses the saying "the best camera is the one you have with you" but wants to push it further.
- [2017-06-28] On a phone the sensor and front glass are the only permanent parts of the camera, so lens-adapter systems (clip-on, bumper, case/holster styles) add another optical layer on top.
- [2017-06-28] He's gravitated to the Moment lenses (the new V2) mostly for their focus on quality; they require a dedicated case that acts as the lens mount, and tiny cine lenses mount to it to change field of view, focal length, and capability — an 18mm (a little wider than normal), a super-wide fisheye, and a 10× macro for extreme close-ups.
- [2017-06-28] The case-as-mount design has a real downside: you're restricted to supported phones and must buy a whole new case when you upgrade — but the payoff is precision (the lens sits over the exact center of the sensor every time, with no vignetting in the corners and no loss of sharpness); the lenses are polarized too.
- [2017-06-28] Moment lenses support only a couple of phones, not all — some he expected to be supported aren't, which the company attributes to fit/vignetting/practicality issues rather than arbitrary limits.
- [2017-06-28] He previously dismissed phone lens adapters as a cheap DSLR alternative, but now accepts that's exactly what they are — you can never match DSLR quality (a DSLR will always be better), but the phone setup will always be smaller and cheaper.
- [2017-06-28] Video adds a whole dimension over photos — camera movement — and after a point handheld shake is unavoidable, so leveling up smartphone video means leveling up stabilization.
- [2017-06-28] The DJI Osmo Mobile is "pretty much the standard" for smartphone stabilization — purpose-built for a phone, with handle controls that pan/tilt when connected, letting the operator just point, shoot, and move while the gimbal smooths out handheld twitches; useful for video or even live streams.
- [2017-06-28] The Osmo Mobile can't handle the extra weight of also adding Moment lenses, so you can't run the Osmo, phone, and lenses together; a bigger gimbal built for real cameras (like the Zhiyun Crane) is needed, and a GorillaPod adapter let him mount his phone to it "if you're feeling daring."
- [2017-06-28] Gear isn't everything — strategy and your existing photography/video knowledge matter too (worth a separate video) — but the point stands: a dedicated camera is bigger and will do a better quality job every time, while phone cameras are always smaller, cheaper, and at your side.
- [2017-06-28] Advice to aspiring creators: to the constant inbox questions of "is this gear/lens enough to start a YouTube channel," the answer is basically yes — if you can watch this video on a device, you probably already have something that shoots video, so just start doing it.
