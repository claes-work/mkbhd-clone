# Google+ Re-design and WhiteSpace Rant! (@mkbhd, 2012-04-12)

## Summary
A light video on the Google+ redesign and the internet's running joke about the enormous empty
column it left on wide screens — he runs through the meme's best uses (sticky notes, a mini Facebook
window, Clippy, "Betty White space") before defending the redesign overall. Slight on its own, but it
carries a **throwaway prediction at the very end of the audio that turns out to be the sharpest thing
in it**: that the white space is future ad inventory.

## Key claims
- [2012-04-12] **The prediction, delivered as a post-outro aside**: "**it's going to be ad space in like
  six months. I give it six months.**" A second instance in six days of reading an unreleased Google
  surface for its future ad load, after "Google putting ads in it is also definitely the future" about
  Project Glass ([[2012-04-06-yt-_hRCAg9jFu8]]).
- [2012-04-12] **Endorses the redesign against the reflex to reject change**: "a lot of people kind of
  tend to reject change [but] a lot of people actually really like this update… it's overall a very very
  clean redesign and I really like it." Column layout, left-hand icon rail, centre timeline, chat panel.
- [2012-04-12] **Reports why he personally didn't notice the problem** — a browsing-habit disclosure:
  "I didn't really notice this for a while because **I don't use my browser maximized**, so I didn't even
  really notice there was a white space until I saw screenshots of it and I was told to maximize."
- [2012-04-12] **Generalizes the complaint rather than singling out Google**: "plenty of websites have a
  white space issue when you run them in a full maximized web browser on a high resolution screen —
  like Facebook."
- [2012-04-12] **Platform advocacy, dated**: opens "hi, my name is Marquez and I'm on Google+… it's an
  awesome social network," and notes he "spent some time on my cover photo." Consistent with the 2011
  platform allocation (Google+ and Twitter active).
- [2012-04-12] Notes the chat panel and cover photo as Facebook-derived features, without treating
  borrowing as a flaw.
