# RETRO TECH: MACINTOSH (@mkbhd, 2019-12-02)

## Summary
An episode of Marques's documentary series "Retro Tech," this time on the original 1984 Apple Macintosh — the computer that came out nine years before he was born and that he frames not as the first personal computer but as the one that changed how people think about what computers are and can do. Marques opens solo, unboxing an original Mac (user guide, Apple stickers, two floppy disks — "A Guided Tour of Macintosh" and the system disk, a brown 1980s keyboard, the built-in carry handle), noting it has no internal memory and boots only from the system floppy, then powering it up to "Welcome to Macintosh." The documentary body interleaves his narration with several guest voices: tech historians/commentators (Humphrey, Ken Segall, Ackerman, Panitz) trace the pre-Apple, room-sized-computer era and IBM's dominance, the arrival of the microprocessor, Apple's garage origins, and the David-vs-Goliath "1984" Super Bowl ad (Ridley Scott, Orwell takeoff, IBM as Big Brother). Fellow creator iJustine walks Marques through pre-Mac hardware at a Brooklyn warehouse (Apple II ~1977, the RadioShack Tandy that outsold Apple at $600, the command-line IBM PC ~1981 with no mouse, the "portable" Compaq 286), landing on the Mac as the machine that popularized the GUI. Ken Segall (self-described Creative Director of Apple for ~17 years) speaks to Jobs's marketing genius and the "have the computer introduce itself" launch moment. Bill Nye joins a "Dope or Nope" software segment (Earth Plot, Asteroids, Mac Surgeon), and artist Pinot Ichwandardi shows MacPaint work he still makes today. Marques closes on the Mac's legacy — that a huge chunk of humanity knows computing as a mouse, keyboard, screen, and OS thanks in major part to the 1984 Mac.

## Key claims
- [2019-12-02] The 1984 Macintosh was not the first personal computer, but it was the one that changed the way people think about what computers are and can do, and one of the machines that helped define what Apple became. (Marques)
- [2019-12-02] The original Mac had no internal memory/storage; it could not start up without the system floppy disk, and everything was loaded into a small amount of RAM — swapping disks was required to move between the system, games, and other software. (Marques; echoed by iJustine)
- [2019-12-02] Pre-Apple, computers were room-sized and found mainly in the military and scientific institutes, operated by specialists in white lab coats, often without screens; IBM ("International Business Machines") was the biggest computer company but had no concept of a home computer. (guests — Humphrey, Segall, Ackerman)
- [2019-12-02] The microprocessor, pioneered in the early 1970s, shrank the computer's "brain" to micro-size, enabling smaller, faster, and cheaper hardware and making a mainstream home computer possible. (guest — Humphrey)
- [2019-12-02] Apple began as Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and Ronald Wayne — hobbyists in a club, building janky prototypes in a garage; the Apple I was essentially just a circuit board, and companies like HP dismissed the idea that people would want a computer at home. (guests — Humphrey, Panitz)
- [2019-12-02] Walkthrough of pre-Mac machines: the Apple II (~1977) was Apple's first push into personal computers; the RadioShack Tandy outsold Apple's 1977 computer because it was $600; the first IBM PC (~1981) was command-line only with no mouse; the Compaq Portable 286 folded its keyboard up and had a luggage handle — this was the era of the "clones" before the 1984 Mac. (guest — iJustine)
- [2019-12-02] The 1984 Mac was one of the first computers with a mouse and deliberately had no arrow keys, to force users to learn the mouse — an early instance of Apple killing off a feature and later bringing it back. (guest — iJustine)
- [2019-12-02] The Mac made the graphical user interface popular; metaphors like dragging a file to a trash can were among the first of their kind, and the setup is essentially the same one used today (Mac OS, Apple menu). (Marques; with iJustine)
- [2019-12-02] Apple has a long pattern of killing off ports/features it decides people no longer need — floppy drive, headphone jack, FireWire, CD drive, full-size USB. (iJustine; Marques)
- [2019-12-02] Apple was positioned as the "little David" against Goliath IBM; the "1984" Super Bowl commercial, directed by Ridley Scott, was a takeoff on Orwell's novel that likened IBM to Big Brother and ended with a woman hammering the screen — "you'll see why 1984 won't be like '1984.'" (guests — Humphrey, Ackerman)
- [2019-12-02] Ken Segall, describing himself as Creative Director of Apple for ~17 years, said Jobs was exceptionally smart but that his marketing passion and talent set him apart from any CEO Segall had worked with, and that Jobs believed every product had to tell a story. (guest — Ken Segall)
- [2019-12-02] At the launch, Jobs had the Macintosh introduce itself on stage — a "master showman" moment meant to say "we're different." (guests — Segall, Panitz)
- [2019-12-02] The original Mac shipped with 128K (kilobytes) of memory; its limited internal storage left room for third-party developers to create a range of software. (Marques; with Bill Nye)
- [2019-12-02] Software demoed on "Dope or Nope" with Bill Nye: Earth Plot (a slow Google-Earth-style mapper — rated dope despite load time), Asteroids (dope), and Mac Surgeon (a higher-learning-curve surgery game — rated nope). (Marques; with Bill Nye)
- [2019-12-02] Jobs got the Macintosh into the hands of influencers/"cool people" — gifting one to John Lennon's son Sean, and Andy Warhol used one in an art installation — to build buzz. (guest — Humphrey)
- [2019-12-02] The Mac bundled software tools like MacWrite and MacPaint; MacPaint made creative work orders of magnitude easier and is "absolutely a straight line to Photoshop." (guest — Ackerman)
- [2019-12-02] Artist Pinot Ichwandardi still uses MacPaint today; his works have gone viral, including a pixel animation of Donald Glover's "This is America." (Marques; with the artist)
- [2019-12-02] The Mac's legacy is that Jobs put a computer in everyone's home and changed the computer from an industrial tool into something personal — "like a pair of eyeglasses," useful but reflecting who you are. (guests — Bill Nye, Ackerman, Panitz)
- [2019-12-02] Marques's closing thesis: while a new generation is growing up knowing computing only as touching and swiping a screen, a gigantic chunk of humanity knows computers as a mouse, a keyboard, a screen, and an OS — thanks in major part to the 1984 Mac and the vision behind it. (Marques)
