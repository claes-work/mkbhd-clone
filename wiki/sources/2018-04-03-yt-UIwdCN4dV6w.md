# Dope Tech: Camera Robots! (@mkbhd, 2018-04-03)

## Summary
A Dope Tech episode built entirely around a field visit to Motorized Precision in Portland, Oregon, maker of what Marques calls "cinema robots" — programmable robotic arms that hold and move a camera with a precision "that a human could never do with any tools that exist now." He frames it against familiar human-operated moves (dolly, Steadicam, slider, crane) that you can usually identify on sight, then argues the robot's appeal is doing the shots you *can't* identify — the super-precise, difficult, non-CGI moves seen in tech commercials, car videos and music videos. The "dope tech" is twofold: the hardware (the Kira robot) plus the software (MP Studio) that programs it. He details Kira's specs, the Xbox-controller-plus-keyframes workflow, the motorized focus/iris/zoom gears for manual lenses, and the deep RED-camera integration, then shoots several demo moves himself (an orbit shot, a phone-on-a-table slider that snaps focus, an over-the-top-of-a-laptop spin, and a harder shot framed on himself), before switching to a smaller/lighter sister robot, Mia, running on a motorized track for a walking tracking shot. He closes on the economics — these robots are generally rented, not bought, for specific commercial/music-video shoots — and floats his own production ambitions ("could probably fit something like this in our studio, wink wink"; a Phantom Flex slow-mo camera on the arm).

## Key claims
- [2018-04-03] For this episode he flew to Portland, Oregon — home of Motorized Precision — to look at their camera robots, which he classes as "cinema robots" and, "as someone that's into video at all," absolutely dope tech.
- [2018-04-03] (paraphrase) Most conventional camera moves are recognizable by the tool used — he calls out dolly cam, Steadicam, slider and crane on-screen; the robot's value is executing precise, difficult, non-CGI shots that a human "could never do with any tools that exist now," hence the company name Motorized Precision.
- [2018-04-03] The dope tech here is twofold: the hardware (the robots) and MP Studio, the software that controls what they do.
- [2018-04-03] Kira spec rundown (paraphrase): fully metal; extends up to nine feet tall; has a couple of fully articulating axes; can move up to nine feet per second in any direction while carrying up to 40 pounds.
- [2018-04-03] The robot is driven with an Xbox controller that has enough buttons to hit every axis — forward/back, up/down, pan left/right, tilt up/down, and roll clockwise/counterclockwise; once positions are set, you move it "through space and time" between them using keyframes in MP Studio.
- [2018-04-03] For manual lenses (which he assumes you're probably using at this level), the robot includes motorized gears that control focus, iris and zoom during the move.
- [2018-04-03] With a RED camera connected to the robot you also get a live preview of what you're pointed at, remote start/stop, and "all your RED [camera] controls" — changing resolution, shutter speed, compression ratio, white balance, etc.
- [2018-04-03] (paraphrase) Once armed, you hit record on the camera and run Kira; it plays back the programmed move at the chosen speed — slow and smooth or fast — and three meters per second is "really fast," so "you gotta know exactly what you're doing to keep it safe."
- [2018-04-03] He credits Sean Brown, CEO of Motorized Precision, for showing him the robots, the software ropes, and example shots.
- [2018-04-03] Demo 1 — orbit shot: a set of keyframes highlighting the same subject from different angles, with the subject kept perfectly in focus at every stop; the result "looks ridiculous," almost CGI, but is real, with motion blur purely dependent on the chosen shutter speed.
- [2018-04-03] Demo 2 — phone-on-a-table slider with a twist: starts slow like a normal slider, then moves in quick and nails instant focus on the phone's camera; the move is repeatable exactly as many times as you want, so you can re-shoot with different phones/items and stack/opacity the clips. He says the beauty is how close to the table the rig gets without touching it, with no shake or wobble.
- [2018-04-03] Demo 3 — up over the top of a laptop, spin around, and down the other side; simple but mind-blowing on screen, and he believes with more time it could be made one continuous float-spin-descend move — "pretty sure that's next to impossible without this robot."
- [2018-04-03] Demo 4 — a harder shot framed on himself: the difficulty is that he's "a human not a robot," and holding himself in the exact same spot long enough to set keyframes and nail focus every take is hard; several unused takes ended with the phone slightly out of focus because he'd moved.
- [2018-04-03] They then broke out a different, smaller/lighter but "equally impressive" Motorized Precision robot called Mia — mounted on a "massive motorized track system" — for a walking tracking shot, matching his walking pace to the robot's slide and stopping so the arm "swings past my head instead of through my head."
- [2018-04-03] (paraphrase) Older iterations of this kind of robot control required literally entering coordinates into an Excel spreadsheet to make the robot move, so MP Studio is "miles better."
- [2018-04-03] Economics: "generally people don't buy these robots, they get rented for certain shoots" like the commercials and music videos discussed.
- [2018-04-03] He floats taking it "to the next level" with a slow-motion camera — imagining a Phantom Flex on the arm swinging around super fast for "absolutely incredible" shots — and hints at wanting one for his own studio ("could probably fit something like this in our studio, wink wink").
