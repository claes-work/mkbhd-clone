# The Worst Product I've Ever Reviewed... For Now (@mkbhd, 2024-04-14)

## Summary
Marques's review of the **Humane AI Pin**, a $700 standalone wearable AI computer (plus a
$24/month subscription) that clips to your clothing via strong magnets, has its own cellular
connection and phone number, and does not pair to a smartphone at all. He structures the video
deliberately in two halves — first what the device *is* and is *supposed* to do (voice queries to
"the Cloud," multimodal camera vision, back-and-forth translation, a green 720p laser projector
that turns your palm into a screen with a learn-it gesture UI), then what actually using it for a
week was like. His verdict is blunt: in its current state it is **"the new worst product I think
I've ever reviewed."** The hardware craftsmanship is genuinely impressive (dense aluminum, hot-swap
battery "boosters," a well-made charging case), but almost everything the software does is slow,
frequently wrong (AI hallucinations, wrong sports schedules, a wrong eclipse date), hot to the
touch, and plagued by terrible, inconsistent battery life that forces constant babysitting. His
central thesis is that **"smartphones are OP"** (overpowered): everything the Pin does, a modern
phone does better, faster, easier, and in higher quality, and because the Pin deliberately refuses
to connect to your phone it can never know the things about you that make an assistant actually
useful. He theorizes the no-phone-pairing choice — like Vision Pro's — is the device being "a
victim of its future ambition," hamstringing the present to protect an eventual standalone future.
He closes by telling viewers not to buy it and to **never buy a product on the promise of future
updates**, while still respecting the team for attempting something genuinely new.

## Key claims
- [2024-04-14] Overall verdict: the Humane AI Pin is, in its current state, the **worst product
  he's ever reviewed** — "bad at almost everything it does, basically all the time." Nobody should
  buy it right now, and you should probably skip it.
- [2024-04-14] Pricing/model: **$700 hardware plus a $24/month subscription** for the data,
  services, and online storage. Colors: matte black (cheapest), or white / black-and-silver for
  $100 more.
- [2024-04-14] It is a **fully standalone device** — it does not pair to a smartphone, has its own
  internet connection and its own phone number, and there is **no app**, only the "Humane Center"
  website portal.
- [2024-04-14] Hardware is the best part "by a lot": solid, dense aluminum; very strong magnets; a
  hot-swappable battery "booster" that magnetizes through your clothing (you get two); a well-made
  USB-C charging case and a desktop charging puck. "A lot of effort was put into the craftsmanship."
- [2024-04-14] Interaction model: no wake word (mic only listens while you touch it); voice queries
  are sent to "the Cloud" and read back; it's **multimodal** (camera can describe a scene); it can
  remember notes, take photos and up to 15-second videos, and do live two-way translation (its best
  tech-demo feature). A **class-2 laser projector** paints a green 720p UI on your palm, navigated
  by a learn-it 3D gesture system (roll to move, pinch to select, fist to go back, push to go
  deeper) — "borderline magic" the first time, but fidgety, inaccurate, and slow.
- [2024-04-14] Software failures: most requests round-trip to the Cloud so it's **slow**; it's
  **often wrong** (AI hallucination), e.g. it said the next eclipse was 2044, gave the wrong next
  Nets game/opponent, and told him to "use the voice command feature" instead of answering a traffic
  query. Server timeouts happen on poor connections. It **cannot set a timer or an alarm.**
- [2024-04-14] Battery life is "really bad" and inconsistent — he saw it drain a full booster in ~2
  hours doing almost nothing, and last ~4 hours under heavy use — requiring charging multiple times
  per day. The device runs **constantly warm** on your chest and has overheated for no clear reason;
  he blames the inefficiency of inductive wireless charging through fabric.
- [2024-04-14] Weight: it's well-made but "just a little too heavy" — it visibly sags on lighter
  clothing — "in the same way that Vision Pro is just a bit too heavy." Aluminum over plastic costs
  you here. The notification light is placed in a corner that sits under where a US seat belt goes.
- [2024-04-14] Media and ecosystem are weak: photos "look pretty bad," videos worse (noisy, squarish,
  15-second max); the sync hub randomly stops working; the projector isn't readable outdoors or far
  from the hand (physics). **There are no apps** — you can sync only Apple, Google, Microsoft, and
  Tidal (the exclusive music partner). No Uber, Spotify, WhatsApp, calendar, or Gmail.
- [2024-04-14] Central thesis — **"smartphones are OP" (overpowered):** everything the Pin does, a
  modern smartphone does better, faster, easier, and in higher quality. Even sending a photo is
  painful (it sends a *link* to view the image in the web portal rather than the image itself).
- [2024-04-14] Why the Pin is structurally handicapped: a good assistant "needs to know everything
  about you," and your phone already knows a lot — but the Pin doesn't talk to your phone, so it's
  at a massive disadvantage. Because it has its own number, texts/calls made from it never share the
  thread with your phone; "that gap is always gonna be there."
- [2024-04-14] Theory on the no-pairing choice: Humane wants the Pin to be a powerful standalone
  device someday, so it won't set the precedent of phone-pairing now only to remove it later — "this
  device as it exists today is hamstrung by its ideal future version of itself." Explicit parallel to
  **Vision Pro**, which also doesn't pair to an iPhone; "this thing is a victim of its future ambition."
- [2024-04-14] The rare good moments: dictating a note/reminder hands-free while driving (it saved a
  "cool ABC outro idea" that was waiting for him at the studio), and frictionless chest-mounted
  first-person video. The one person who might most consider it is someone who wants **as little
  screen time as possible** — sometimes that's him — but the friction undermines even that.
- [2024-04-14] Buying rule stated as a general principle: **"never buy a product based on the future
  promise of updates to it."** Humane has shared a roadmap (e.g. number sharing) but with no dates.
- [2024-04-14] Despite the harsh verdict, he explicitly **respects the attempt**: "we don't get a
  lot of totally new stuff," and he values first-generation products existing at all.
