# The Last (and First) Folding Phone! (@mkbhd, 2019-07-20)

## Summary
Marques takes a studio hands-on look at the Royole FlexPai, framing it as the first and only folding phone to actually ship in 2019 given that the Samsung Galaxy Fold kept slipping its relaunch and Huawei (the Mate X) was "going through some things." Having only seen the device under tightly controlled conditions at CES in January, he now evaluates it like any other phone that passes through his studio. His central thesis is that folding the screen on the outside is the wrong approach: the delicate flexible OLED is permanently exposed, the components can't fold flat so the device is chunky (he likens it to a Seinfeld/Costanza wallet), the fold radius stays far greater than zero, and the outside-fold geometry produces two creases rather than one. He is especially critical of the hinge, which he describes as an accordion-shaped rubber cover screwed in at the sides — noisy, hack-like, and of dubious long-term durability with no rated fold count, unlike the Galaxy Fold's engineered (if flawed) 200,000-fold claim. He walks through the FlexPai's Water OS software quirks (a persistent nav-bar button to toggle the back screen, front/back screens remembering separate app states, a dual-viewfinder camera with a "baby mode," and a comically large number of auto-rotate orientations), notes the phone has no rear-only cameras so you can't shoot subjects other than yourself without closing it, and flags real usability and trust problems: split volume buttons, non-button fingerprint reader, no headphone jack, no Google Play services/Play Store, sparse English, an odd instruction to only charge while unfolded, and privacy concerns about inserting a SIM. He concedes flashes of brilliance — a screen folded fully backwards still working is genuinely impressive — but concludes the FlexPai mostly made him miss the Galaxy Fold. Two lessons: being first doesn't matter if the product is bad, and (while hinges/screens remain this fragile) folding in beats folding out; he cites the Huawei Mate X and upcoming Motorola Razr as reference points.

## Key claims
- [2019-07-20] The Royole FlexPai is the first and only folding phone to actually ship in 2019, since the Galaxy Fold never officially launched and Huawei's Mate X was delayed.
- [2019-07-20] The CES January showing barely qualified as a hands-on because Royole took the device back every few seconds to demonstrate "correct" use.
- [2019-07-20] Folding the screen on the outside is the wrong approach: the flexible OLED (the most delicate part) is always exposed and uncovered.
- [2019-07-20] Because components can't fold completely flat, the phone is thicker, the fold radius stays well above zero (more a sharp curve than a fold), and it's too chunky for most pockets.
- [2019-07-20] The hinge is a hack — accordion-shaped rubber screwed in at the sides that isn't smooth, makes noises when folded wrong, and raises durability doubts (wear, sun, scratches, ripping).
- [2019-07-20] Unlike the Galaxy Fold's engineered hinge with a claimed 200,000-fold robot test, the FlexPai's mechanism appears to have no rated fold count and doesn't seem more durable.
- [2019-07-20] The outside-fold design produces two creases (one on either side of the hinge) rather than the Galaxy Fold's single slight crease.
- [2019-07-20] Royole marketed the device as a "three screen" phone (front, back, side), but Marques rejects the awkward sliding-shortcut middle strip as a real third screen.
- [2019-07-20] Water OS treats the front and back screens as separate UIs, remembering each side's app state independently (e.g., Settings open on one side, Clock on the other).
- [2019-07-20] The dual-camera layout lets both front and back act as viewfinders, and a "baby mode" replaces the back screen with cartoons to get babies to look at the camera.
- [2019-07-20] There are no rear-only cameras — all cameras face the user — so you can't photograph subjects other than yourself unless you close the phone.
- [2019-07-20] Software has improved since CES (especially animations) but still isn't fluid; the fold-in-half motion of grabbing the screen from both sides remains incredibly awkward.
- [2019-07-20] Usability/trust drawbacks: split volume buttons around a non-button fingerprint reader, no headphone jack, Water OS with little English, no Google Play services or Play Store, and setup instructions to only charge while unfolded.
- [2019-07-20] It's genuinely impressive that a screen folded fully backwards still works and stays responsive.
- [2019-07-20] Two takeaways: being first doesn't matter if it sucks, and — while hinges/screens are this fragile — folding in is better than folding out (Mate X may look better but fold-out isn't the way; Motorola Razr's fold-in is another good approach).
