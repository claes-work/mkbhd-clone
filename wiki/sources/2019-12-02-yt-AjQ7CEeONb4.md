# RETRO TECH: WALKMAN (@mkbhd, 2019-12-02)

## Summary
An episode of Marques's "Retro Tech" documentary series, in which he explores the 1979 Sony TPS-L2 Walkman — the first portable music player — despite (as he stresses) never having used or even seen one before. The documentary format interleaves Marques's first-person narration and hands-on reactions with talking-head clips from several guests: tech/culture commentators introduced as Seth, Lisa, Alex, and Americus supply the historical context, cultural analysis, and marketing story; a cassette collector (Ryan) opens the cold open; Toshio Asai, an engineer on the original Walkman development team at Sony in Tokyo, is interviewed (in Japanese, paraphrased by Marques); fellow YouTuber Judner "UrAvgConsumer" joins a repair attempt; and the Gregory Brothers help run a durability test. Marques unboxes the original Walkman (leather case, flimsy lightweight headphones, AA batteries, tactile "immensely satisfying" buttons) and plays a Beastie Boys tape. Guest commentary traces the origin (Sony co-founder Masaru Ibuka, an opera-loving audiophile, wanting his clunky journalist's cassette recorder made portable; engineers stripped out the recording components until only playback remained), the initial backlash (safety fears, a 1982 Woodbridge, New Jersey ban on wearing one while crossing streets, "anti-social" panic), and Sony's marketing fix (dual his-and-hers headphone jacks, a "hot line" button, celebrity/"influence marketing," "revolution in the streets" ads, color and sport SKUs). The piece connects the Walkman to the mixtape era (cassette sales outselling vinyl three years after US release), the exercise boom, the lightweight-headphone revolution, and its eventual displacement by CDs and the 2001 iPod. Marques's failed repair (with Judner) and a Sports Walkman frisbee stress test (with the Gregory Brothers, which the rugged sport model largely survives) bookend the guest history. He closes on the through-line: the Walkman established that technology can be with you all the time, and "they don't make 'em like they used to."

## Key claims
- [2019-12-02] Sony introduced the TPS-L2 Walkman in 1979 as the first-ever portable music player, changing the music game; the video marks the Walkman's 40th anniversary. (Marques narration)
- [2019-12-02] Roughly half of people alive now were not alive when the Walkman was introduced; Marques himself was born after it and had never used or seen one before this video. (Marques)
- [2019-12-02] The original Walkman shipped with a leather case, flimsy lightweight headphones, and ran on AA batteries, with tactile fast-forward / rewind / play-pause / stop buttons Marques calls "immensely satisfying." (Marques, hands-on)
- [2019-12-02] Before the Walkman, music was innately social and loud — boom boxes were the status symbol ("the bigger it was, the cooler you were") and portable cassette players that existed were VCR-sized, chunky, and built for journalists and secretaries recording notes, not for music. (guests — Seth, Lisa)
- [2019-12-02] Sony co-founder Masaru Ibuka, a huge opera-loving audiophile, found using a note-recording cassette player on the road impractical; he tasked his team, who stripped away the recording gears and components until only a small, sleek playback device remained, yielding the 1979 Walkman. (guests — Seth, Lisa)
- [2019-12-02] Marques contacted Toshio Asai, an engineer on the original Walkman development team at Sony in Tokyo; per Marques's paraphrase, the Walkman was built so well that consumers would keep and repair them (rather than discard) even when they broke, back when people were more handy. (Marques paraphrasing guest interview)
- [2019-12-02] A common Walkman failure was the rubber bands inside that turn the tapes stretching or breaking; with correct instructions the repair was theoretically possible, but Marques (with Judner "UrAvgConsumer") failed the attempt — dropping a screw and jamming/destroying the tape — concluding it gave him respect for those who could do it. (Marques + guest, hands-on)
- [2019-12-02] The Walkman drew real backlash as unsafe and anti-social ("the Me Generation gone wild"); Woodbridge, New Jersey passed a 1982 law banning listening to a Walkman while crossing streets, a ban said to still stand. (guests — Seth, Lisa)
- [2019-12-02] Sony turned "bad stares into good stares" with marketing featuring fun, hip, attractive people and prominent celebrities using Walkmen in public — an early form of what is now called influence/influencer marketing (today mostly on Instagram) — with the tagline "there's a revolution in the streets." (guests — Lisa, and others)
- [2019-12-02] To fight the isolation of personal audio, Sony added his-and-hers dual headphone jacks (two people listening at once) and a "hot line" button that let someone speak through the device while automatically lowering the music. (guests, demoed by Marques)
- [2019-12-02] The Walkman's small lightweight headphones (reducing weight roughly tenfold vs. prior big clunky headphones) were key to its success and revolutionized the headphone market, ushering in the age of personal headphones. (guests — Lisa, Alex)
- [2019-12-02] When the Walkman took off the cassette tape took off with it: three years after the Walkman's US release, cassette sales outsold vinyl for the first time; the blank cassette enabled mixtapes, giving people real control over their music and making the mixtape central to high-school culture and courtship. (guests — Lisa, Alex, Americus)
- [2019-12-02] The Walkman's biggest impact was on exercise (running, jogging, aerobics, Jazzercise booming simultaneously), prompting Sony's rugged 1980s Sports Walkman — a "natural product market fit." (guests — Seth, Lisa, Americus)
- [2019-12-02] In a frisbee stress test with the Gregory Brothers, the yellow Sports Walkman held up well for its running/jogging use case — the tape stayed in and playback survived jostling — though the battery clip's clamp force came undone too easily. (Marques + guests, hands-on)
- [2019-12-02] Product variation (colors, AM/FM models, sport models, many SKUs) let Sony make a mass product still feel personal to each owner. (guest — Americus)
- [2019-12-02] The Walkman's appeal faded as CDs rose in the mid-'90s (more capacity, more hi-fi), and the 2001 iPod — your entire library in your pocket vs. one or two cassettes — put the final nail in the coffin; it was digital music that ended the Walkman. (guests — Alex, Lisa)
- [2019-12-02] The Walkman's legacy: it moved music from background into the forefront as central self-expression and created the notion that technology can be with you all the time — the lineage behind not leaving home without our phones and spending hundreds on headphones we wear constantly. (guests — Americus, Seth; echoed by Marques)
- [2019-12-02] Marques's biggest takeaway: the Walkman paved the way for portable audio as we know it — from the internal gears to the bands to the clicky buttons — "they don't make 'em like they used to." (Marques)
