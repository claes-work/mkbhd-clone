# 5G: Explained! (@mkbhd, 2019-08-19)

## Summary
Marques travels from his studio to Providence, Rhode Island — the nearest Verizon 5G city — with a $1,600 Samsung Galaxy S10 5G to test real-world 5G for himself, motivated by the wave of carrier-sponsored "flown-to-Chicago" 5G speed-test videos flooding his sub box. He frames 5G as the fifth generation of wireless network technology whose main theme is speed, built on high-frequency millimeter-wave spectrum, and explains the fundamental physics trade-off: higher frequency means shorter range and poor penetration through trees, walls, and buildings. Standing directly beside a Verizon 5G node (three nodes atop a light pole near Brown University) with clear line of sight, he records blistering download speeds — regularly 700–900+ Mbps, peaking at 1.9 Gbps — and demonstrates near-instant YouTube playback, fast game downloads (PUBG Mobile, Fortnite), and a full HD Stranger Things episode in about four seconds. But he shows the experience degrade sharply the moment he steps behind a brick wall ~75 feet away (speeds cut roughly in half) or walks down the block (constant bouncing between 4G and 5G). He flags three persistent drawbacks even under ideal conditions — upload still capped at 4G speeds (never above 100 Mbps up), ping still at 4G levels, and notable heat plus battery drain (75% to 21% over ~4–5 hours). He also calls out AT&T's "5GE" status-bar labeling as a misleading lie since it is still 4G. His verdict: in 2019 5G is like folding phones — a great glimpse of the future but clearly not ready, cons outweigh pros, and not worth spending extra money on yet, while the long-term vision (VR streaming, autonomous car networks, remote surgery, the end of buffering) depends on an expensive, slow, city-blanketing node rollout that is a big assumption and years off.

## Key claims
- [2019-08-19] 5G is the fifth generation of wireless network technology, and its main theme — like every prior generation — is significantly greater speed and capability than the previous one.
- [2019-08-19] All the big US carriers (Verizon, AT&T, Sprint) are building out 5G networks, one city and location at a time.
- [2019-08-19] Taking advantage of 5G requires a 5G device with 5G radios plus a 5G network; a 4G phone cannot be enabled for 5G via a software update.
- [2019-08-19] AT&T relabeling phones' status bars as "5GE" is misleading because it is still 4G, not 5G — Marques calls it "basically a lie."
- [2019-08-19] 5G (as deployed here) is built on millimeter waves — a new section of very high frequency spectrum, which Marques states as roughly 20 GHz up to near 96 GHz.
- [2019-08-19] Higher-frequency waves have shorter range as a matter of physics — the same reason 5 GHz Wi-Fi travels less far than 2.4 GHz, and microwaves don't cook through a glass wall.
- [2019-08-19] Millimeter-wave 5G travels well only within direct line of sight; even a few hundred feet from the node, trees, walls, and buildings block or disrupt the signal, and rain in the air may potentially be a problem.
- [2019-08-19] Standing right next to a Verizon 5G node with line of sight, downloads were regularly 700–900+ Mbps, often above 1,000 Mbps, peaking at 1.9 Gbps briefly on fast.com.
- [2019-08-19] At those speeds a full HD Stranger Things Netflix episode downloaded in about four seconds — a whole season in roughly 30 seconds.
- [2019-08-19] Stepping ~75 feet from the node but behind a brick wall roughly halved speeds (to ~200–400 Mbps); walking down the block caused frustratingly inconsistent bouncing between 4G and 5G.
- [2019-08-19] Upload speed stayed at 4G levels (never above 100 Mbps up) even when connected to 5G, making dreams like uploading an 8K video in seconds or live-streaming high-res VR impossible at this point.
- [2019-08-19] Ping/latency was still at 4G levels; the goal of getting it to a millisecond or less was not happening in these tests.
- [2019-08-19] The Galaxy S10 5G ran warm to the touch throughout and drained from 75% to 21% battery over ~4–5 hours of testing, even without gaming — which is why 5G phones ship with larger batteries than their 4G counterparts.
- [2019-08-19] The current plan to fix coverage is brute-force: blanketing areas with many lower-powered nodes/cells you switch between quickly, which Marques expects to be incredibly expensive and to take years.
- [2019-08-19] Verdict: in 2019 5G is like folding phones — not ready yet, the cons outweigh the pros, and not worth spending extra money on.
- [2019-08-19] The long-term promise (VR streaming, networks of internet-connected autonomous cars, remote surgery over 5G, the end of video buffering) is bright but hinges on the big assumption that the infrastructure happens and isn't too obtrusive.
