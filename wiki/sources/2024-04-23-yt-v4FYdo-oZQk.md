# How One Small Company Saves Retro Tech (@Waveform, 2024-04-23)

## Summary
A midweek bonus Waveform episode built around a long interview with Cory and Adam, the married co-owners of Retrospekt, a ~40-person Milwaukee company that sources, refurbishes, repairs, and re-manufactures retro tech (Polaroid cameras, Sony Walkmans, VCRs, early-digital cameras, cassette players, watches, vinyl). The panel and guests trace how a college hobby of thrifting Polaroid cameras grew — via a supplier relationship with The Impossible Project — into a bootstrapped consumer-electronics business. The bulk of the substance comes from the two guests; the hosts (introduced as Ellis, Marques, and David) drive the questions and add retro-tech banter. Note: on this episode the interview is led by co-host Ellis (a self-described Brooklyn-based cassette/retro collector and audio engineer), not Marques.

## Key claims
- [2024-04-23] Retrospekt is a consumer-electronics company on three pillars: refurbishing/restoring retro tech, producing some of its own retro tech, and reselling others' newly manufactured retro-adjacent goods (music, film). (guest — Cory/Adam)
- [2024-04-23] The company's origin is tied to Polaroid: after Polaroid announced the end of instant-film manufacturing (~2008; sell-off after ~2005 wind-down references in transcript), instant film spiked on eBay to $30–40 a pack, so as broke college students they thrifted Polaroid cameras that still had film inside. (guest)
- [2024-04-23] They became The Impossible Project's raw-camera supplier, and around 2012 were the biggest global hardware supplier for The Impossible Project; a deal to take over refurbishment/repair (trained by ex-Polaroid factory staff) wrapped in early 2015 and formalized the business (LLC named Retrospekt). (guest)
- [2024-04-23] A core sourcing criterion is that a product must have been mass-produced, because the repair model depends on fusing multiple donor units into one working unit (parts-swapping, plus 3D printing and injection molding where volume supports it). (guest)
- [2024-04-23] They deliberately target items destined for landfill that don't work without a technician, rather than reselling already-working gear with a markup. (guest)
- [2024-04-23] Rust, mold, and UV-yellowed brittle plastic are the main "too far gone" failure modes; they run a full paint booth (partly to coat camera interiors light-tight, since colored plastic is translucent and leaks light). (guest)
- [2024-04-23] Their CP81 is a newly manufactured portable cassette player; factory quality is inconsistent, so units are third-party inspected, then 100% re-inspected and retuned in-house (playhead/azimuth adjustment). A CP82 is targeted within a year; the business is bootstrapped (owner-funded, no VC). (guest)
- [2024-04-23] Modern tech is generally harder to repair than older gear because chips/boards are proprietary and protected; older devices were designed to be opened and serviced. (guest, in response to right-to-repair framing)
- [2024-04-23] They do NOT open CRTs (safety); CRTs sold on their site are dead stock found in a factory. General PSA: don't open guitar amps, CRTs, or washing machines even unplugged — electrocution risk. (guest; PSA echoed by a host — attribution of the host line uncertain)
- [2024-04-23] The company runs ~40 employees (their website said 25; out of date), ~15,000 sq ft plus offsite cold storage holding thousands of units (e.g. Sony Sports Walkmans), 10,000+ SKUs due to many one-off items; roughly 50/50 office-to-floor and 50/50 male/female repair techs. (guest)
- [2024-04-23] Repair and refurbishment are treated as distinct roles (refurb = cosmetic cleaning/polishing; repair = mechanical fixes); most repair techs start in refurb, and full SX-70 / 670 proficiency takes about a year to train. (guest)
- [2024-04-23] Being in the Midwest (Milwaukee) helped via access to local injection molders and paper/packaging manufacturers and a blue-collar labor pool; Polaroid externals are still made in Wisconsin, though some manufacturing has moved elsewhere for volume/consistency and shipping cost. (guest)
- [2024-04-23] Panel/guest thesis: retro tech is less a passing trend than "a door opening" for a new generation hungry for tangible, analog experiences (cassettes, vinyl, film, digicams), which both old and new formats can coexist to serve. (guest, echoed by panel)
- [2024-04-23] Panel banter: a three-head cassette deck has dedicated record and playback heads (vs. a two-head deck's shared head), letting you monitor audio as it's written to tape; the improvement is monitoring capability, not inherently higher fidelity. (panel — corrects the Humane AI Pin's on-air answer)
