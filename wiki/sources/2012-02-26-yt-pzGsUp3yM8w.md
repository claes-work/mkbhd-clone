# Pixel Density: Explained! (@mkbhd, 2012-02-26)

## Summary
**The earliest full technical explainer in the corpus** — a from-first-principles teardown of pixel
density made specifically to answer commenters who said a retina iPad would be "unnecessary… it's
just a tablet." He builds the whole argument on one idea that resolves the question:
**pixel density only matters relative to viewing distance**. Sub-pixels → resolution notation → the
PPI formula → what "retina" actually means → then a walk down the device classes (phone, tablet,
monitor, TV) showing PPI falling as viewing distance grows. Ends with predictions, one of which
turned out squarely wrong.

## Key claims
- [2012-02-26] **The organizing principle**: "the higher pixel density, the better when it's that close
  to your face" — density is judged against how far the device sits from your eye, not in the abstract.
  Stated distances: phone <2 ft, tablet ~3 ft, desktop monitor ~4 ft, TV much further.
- [2012-02-26] **"Retina" defined generically, not as a brand**: "a term dubbed by Apple, is really any
  display with a pixel density so high that the retina of the human eye cannot detect individual pixels
  from a typical viewing distance." He treats it as a measurable threshold rather than marketing.
- [2012-02-26] **The method shown, not assumed**: multiply horizontal × vertical for total pixels,
  multiply the physical dimensions for area, divide. "Here in America, we use pixels per inch, as silly
  as inches are."
- [2012-02-26] **A usable rule of thumb**: "just about any phone display with a pixel density of around
  250 PPI or higher will look excellent from such a close range" — with the note that plenty of
  contemporary phones "come nowhere near that."
- [2012-02-26] **The device-class ladder, with numbers**: iPhone 326 PPI · iPad 2 132 · Xoom and
  Transformer Prime 150 · typical 24" 1080p monitor 91 · 19" 1280×1024 86 · 27" Apple Thunderbolt
  Display 109 · 42" 1080p TV 52.
- [2012-02-26] **Deflates a premium product with the same math** — the notable move: "while you may be
  inclined to believe that your precious Apple Thunderbolt display is superior in clarity, seems very
  sharp, it's really not… putting it right in line with other displays in that size and that price
  range." A perceived-quality claim dismantled by arithmetic.
- [2012-02-26] Sub-pixel primer, including the PenTile caveat — displays with fewer sub-pixels per pixel
  "tend to look a little bit worse when displaying the color that they are missing" — before
  simplifying to RGB for the rest of the explainer.
- [2012-02-26] **Predictions (dated, mixed outcomes — see note)**: high-resolution *tablets* are "the
  real bet" (Transformer Prime TF700T at 1920×1200 announced; a rumored 2560-wide Samsung 11"), the
  iPad 3 rumored at 2048×1536, and — explicitly — **"there have been rumors about a retina display
  MacBook Pro, but I wouldn't put any money on it."**
- [2012-02-26] Verdict on the rumored iPad 3 display: "Overkill? Probably. But will it be beautiful?
  Absolutely."
- [2012-02-26] Nostalgic teaching aside: "I'm sure when you were young, you looked up at the TV up close
  and noticed the red, green, and blue dots that made up the picture. Yeah, you were looking at sub
  pixels there."
