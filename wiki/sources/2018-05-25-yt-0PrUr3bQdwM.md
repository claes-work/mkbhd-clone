# OnePlus 6 Review: Right On the Money! (@mkbhd, 2018-05-25)

## Summary
A full solo review of the OnePlus 6 after ~two weeks of daily use, framed almost entirely as a case study in the *law of diminishing returns* — the point at which added money buys progressively less added value. Marques's thesis is that the OnePlus 6's most important feature is its $529 starting price in a market of $700–$1000 flagships, and that OnePlus works "in the reverse direction": rather than maximizing value, they ask how much price they can cut without sacrificing significant value. He rates the build as genuinely quality (glass-and-metal wedge, clicky buttons, alert slider, headphone jack, dual-SIM, frosted-glass S-curve back), then catalogs the deliberate cost cuts — no expandable storage, a weak single downward-firing speaker (the worst hardware part), a smaller rear fingerprint reader, no wireless charging, and no official IP water-resistance rating. He argues the battery story makes the missing wireless charging a non-issue: a 3300 mAh cell plus near-stock OxygenOS plus a 1080p AMOLED plus a Snapdragon 845 nets him 6+ hours of screen-on time, topped off by Dash Charge, still the fastest 0–100 charging he's seen. He defends the 1080p (2280×1080) 6.3-inch Optic AMOLED as "part strategy, part sacrifice" — worse for sharpness than QHD Galaxy S9 / Pixel 2 XL panels but better for battery and GPU load, and, in his view, the best-looking 1080p OLED in any phone at the time. He praises OxygenOS as fast, clean, and feature-rich while staying close to stock (Android P beta early access is a longevity plus), with his one software gripe being the new swipe gestures, which work but aren't as smooth as the iPhone's. On cameras he lands at "B-plus for quality, A-plus for the price": a 16 MP OIS f/1.7 main that satisfies without wowing, and a secondary 20 MP sensor used mainly for portrait-mode depth rather than telephoto. Verdict: the most polished phone OnePlus has ever made, and "A-plus for the price" — he explicitly refuses to review it like a $900 flagship.

## Key claims
- [2018-05-25] After ~two weeks of daily use the OnePlus 6 is "absolutely daily driver material" and easy to recommend to buyers who know what they're getting into.
- [2018-05-25] The whole review is framed around the *law of diminishing returns* — "a point at which the level of benefits gained is less than the amount of money or energy invested"; each additional ~$300 spent on a phone buys progressively less added value.
- [2018-05-25] The single most important feature of the OnePlus 6 is its price: it starts at $529 in a world of $700/$800/$900/$1000 flagships.
- [2018-05-25] OnePlus works "in the reverse direction" — instead of maximizing value, they ask "how cheap can we make this phone and still make it great," balancing what to cut against what to keep; he thinks the 2018 balance came out as a "balanced budget performance beast."
- [2018-05-25] Build quality is legitimately good: glass-and-metal wedge shape that "feels super solid with a lot of metal," clicky buttons, a firm/clicky alert slider (placed high enough that he initially confused it with the power button), a headphone jack, a dual-SIM tray, and a subtle S-curve visible in the frosted-glass back. Color options: midnight/matte black, glossier "mirror black," and a soft white coming soon.
- [2018-05-25] Deliberate cost cuts: no expandable storage (the tray is dual-SIM, no microSD); a weak single downward-facing speaker — "the worst part of the phone's hardware," tinny at high volume, easy to block by accident, with no earpiece stereo pairing; a smaller rear fingerprint reader (was a full circle, now smaller surface area).
- [2018-05-25] No wireless charging: despite the glass back (which most phones adopt specifically to enable wireless charging, since it doesn't work through metal), OnePlus omitted the wireless coil.
- [2018-05-25] No official IP rating — OnePlus informally says it'll survive daily splashes but does not carry an official IP67/IP68 water-resistance certification.
- [2018-05-25] He argues he doesn't miss wireless charging because the battery combination works: a 3300 mAh battery + near-stock Android (OxygenOS) + 1080p AMOLED + Snapdragon 845 with 8 GB RAM nets him "six plus hours every single day of screen-on time," plus Dash Charge — "still the fastest zero to 100 charging I've ever seen in a phone."
- [2018-05-25] The display is a 6.3-inch 1080p (2280×1080) Optic AMOLED with a notch — "part strategy, part sacrifice": the lower resolution is "hugely beneficial for battery life" and performance (fewer pixels = less GPU load = smoother), at the cost of sharpness versus QHD panels on the Galaxy S9 and Pixel 2 XL.
- [2018-05-25] He'd rather have a great 1080p OLED than an average QHD one: citing the Pixel's QHD quality-control troubles, and that "a great 1440p panel is not cheap or easy to find," he calls this "the best looking 1080p OLED in any phone right now" — bright, colorful, with multiple color-profile modes.
- [2018-05-25] Best part of the display is OnePlus's notch handling: a display setting can hide the notch anytime (black OLED pixels, no lost real estate), and by default — unlike the iPhone — apps like YouTube are not cut off; full-screen mode that lets the notch intrude is opt-in per app (contrasted with the old Essential Phone that cut into apps).
- [2018-05-25] OxygenOS is fast and smooth, feature-rich yet close to stock: fast face unlock, a gaming mode that blocks notification interruptions, a new (non-always-on) raise-to-wake ambient display, and the "Shelf." He values that he can keep his setup clean.
- [2018-05-25] Longevity signal: the OnePlus 6 is one of ~six phones (alongside the Pixel) included in the Android P beta program — a good sign for update timing; likely better-updated than Samsung/LG many months out, though a Pixel is still first in line.
- [2018-05-25] His one software dislike is the new swipe gestures: bug-free and intuitive but "just not smooth" compared to the iPhone's very responsive gestures — he sticks with the buttons.
- [2018-05-25] Camera verdict: "B-plus" for smartphone camera quality but "A-plus for the price." The main is a 16 MP OIS f/1.7 shooter — plenty of detail, low noise, accurate/saturated colors, a slight bias toward overexposing, good-enough dynamic range; he's "satisfied," not "blown away."
- [2018-05-25] The second rear camera is not a telephoto — it's a 20 MP sensor with no OIS, used mainly for portrait-mode depth data; he calls portrait mode "okay… decent, not great."
- [2018-05-25] Recurring theme of the whole review: the OnePlus 6 is either "knocking at the door of those flagship smartphones or… one small step behind," while definitively "beating any other five/six hundred dollar Android phone."
- [2018-05-25] Spec recap: Snapdragon 845, 6 or 8 GB RAM, up to 128 GB storage, 16 MP camera, 3300 mAh battery, USB-C, fast charging, headphone jack, dual-SIM, huge AMOLED.
- [2018-05-25] Final verdict: "both literally and figuratively the most polished phone OnePlus has ever made"; it would have been easy to review it like a $900 flagship and bash it for missing wireless charging or an A-plus camera, but the keep-versus-cut choices balanced out to a quality phone — "a great phone for the money, A-plus for the price."
