# Dope Tech: Lightning Headphones! (@mkbhd, 2016-07-14)

## Summary
Ahead of a rumored iPhone 7 that drops the 3.5mm headphone jack, Marques examines the emerging category of Lightning headphones to test whether a digital-only future would actually be any good. He reviews two pairs — the entry-level Philips Fidelio and the higher-end Audeze Sine — both of which route audio over the Lightning port and carry their own built-in DAC (digital-to-analog converter) rather than relying on the phone's internal DAC. His through-line is even-handed but cautiously pro-digital: a dedicated, headphone-matched DAC delivers a real, audible improvement (better instrument separation, tighter bass, more power for demanding drivers), so the advantage is "kind of real" — but he flags the open problems of a jackless phone (you can't charge and listen at once without going wireless) and notes that all current Lightning headphones are premium/expensive, leaving the cheap-earbud question unanswered. He points viewers to his companion video on the digital-vs-analog debate and frames the iPhone 7, a few months out, as the moment the answers arrive.

## Key claims
- [2016-07-14] Rumors point to the next iPhone (and a couple of other phones) shipping with no headphone jack at all, and the recent influx of Lightning headphones from many manufacturers makes a jackless iPhone look pretty likely.
- [2016-07-14] Removing the jack leaves Apple's bundled in-box earbuds three paths: make them Lightning headphones, make them wireless earbuds, or stop including them — and he hopes they don't drop them.
- [2016-07-14] Bluetooth headphones can be pretty good but are easier to lose when wireless and a bit more expensive, which is why many expect Apple to bundle Lightning headphones rather than wireless.
- [2016-07-14] The Philips Fidelio is the simplest implementation of a Lightning headphone — a normal-looking on-ear pair that connects via the Lightning port instead of the jack.
- [2016-07-14] Because Lightning headphones carry their own built-in DAC (inside the Fidelio's cups, not the cable), going through the Lightning port means you no longer rely on the iPhone's built-in DAC.
- [2016-07-14] The Fidelio is a little bass-heavy but sounds pretty good, with a side volume rocker and a large play/pause button spanning the entire right earcup.
- [2016-07-14] The Audeze Sine is a big step up in price and build — heavier, with metal hinges and earcup holders, pseudo-leather earcup backs and a soft leather headband — and it puts its built-in DAC in the cable.
- [2016-07-14] The Sine ships with two cables: a Lightning cable with the built-in DAC, and a plain 3.5mm cable with no DAC that forces you to use the phone's DAC — letting you directly A/B the difference.
- [2016-07-14] High-end headphones with big beefy drivers can't be powered to their full potential by a phone's DAC; over plain 3.5mm the volume maxes out only "barely decent" and the sound is flat and weak, "like listening from behind a thin wall."
- [2016-07-14] Switching the Sine to the bundled Lightning DAC, purpose-built for those headphones, gives audibly better instrument separation, better volume, tighter bass and more pronounced mids and highs.
- [2016-07-14] The advantage of Lightning headphones' built-in custom DAC is real, but it still doesn't solve how to charge and listen to music simultaneously — you'd need wireless headphones for that.
- [2016-07-14] Both reviewed pairs are premium/expensive, so it remains an open question how cheap a basic pair of Lightning earbuds could be — and the iPhone 7, a few months away, will provide the answers.
