# The Ioniq 5 XRT: "My Favorite Electric Car *Right Now*" (@AutoFocus, 2026-01-29)

## Summary
Marques reviews the **Hyundai Ioniq 5 XRT**, the off-road/all-terrain variant of the
Ioniq 5, driving it through a heavy New Jersey snowstorm (a foot of snow, more coming).
His framing: EVs have matured to the point where there are now **"specialty EVs"** —
little variations of each model (the sporty Ioniq 5 N, this rugged 5 XRT) — where in
2022 nearly all EVs just got compared to each other because they were so similar. The
XRT's big selling point is that it costs barely more than a regular premium Ioniq 5
(~$46k, within $2–3k of the standard car, possibly haggle-able), yet adds a genuinely
useful aesthetic + capability package for cold/snowy climates. He walks the exterior
(camo-textured dark front bumper, red recovery hooks, all-terrain tires with meaty
sidewalls, ~1-inch lift), the familiar interior (capacitive HVAC buttons he still
dislikes, sliding center console, mediocre wireless charger), and the terrain drive
modes (snow/mud/sand). Two honest weaknesses: **range** (rated 260 mi, realistically
220–230 in winter on all-terrain tires, showing ~200 in extreme cold) and a
**surprising NACS charging disappointment** — at a Tesla Supercharger the facelifted
NACS-port car peaked in the 50s–60s kW, slower than it did with an adapter at Electrify
America. He crowd-sources other owners' NACS-at-Supercharger experiences. Verdict: not a
serious locking-diff off-roader (more an aesthetics + light-capability package, like a
RAV4 GR Sport), but genuinely fun and the best-looking Ioniq to him — ideal not for road
trips but for people in cold/pothole-heavy climates. Marques-solo.

## Key claims
- [2026-01-29] EVs have matured into **"specialty EVs"** — 2026 has enough variety that
  models spawn purpose-built variants (Ioniq 5 N for performance, 5 XRT for
  all-terrain), unlike ~2022 when EVs were few and largely interchangeable. (Marques)
- [2026-01-29] The 5 XRT's strongest asset is price: ~$46k, only ~$2–3k above a regular
  premium Ioniq 5 sitting next to it on the lot — a small premium for the aesthetic +
  capability upgrade. Dual-motor AWD, 320 hp, 446 lb-ft, 0–60 in 4.8s, ~260 mi rated
  range. (Marques)
- [2026-01-29] Real-world winter range is well below the 260 rating — realistically
  220–230 mi, dropping toward ~200 in extreme cold, worsened by the all-terrain tires.
  He frames cold as the #2 range factor after driving style/speed. (Marques)
- [2026-01-29] **NACS surprise:** at a Tesla Supercharger the NACS-equipped facelifted
  car never got near 100 kW (peaked ~50–60 kW); it actually charged *faster* with an
  adapter at Electrify America, and pre-facelift cars charged faster still with no
  adapter at EA. He'd assumed NACS would be a big speed improvement — it's great for
  availability/reliability but "hasn't always been fast." Asks other non-Tesla NACS
  owners (Rivian, Lucid Gravity) to report their experiences. (Marques)
- [2026-01-29] The XRT is an aesthetics + light-capability package, **not** a serious
  off-roader — no adjustable air suspension, no locking differentials; ~1-inch lift plus
  snow/mud/sand terrain modes. He compares it to the **Toyota RAV4 GR Sport** (looks +
  minor driving tweaks). (Marques)
- [2026-01-29] Still dislikes the capacitive HVAC buttons; the sliding center console
  and heated wheel/seats are pluses; the wireless charger "still doesn't work the best."
  (Marques)
- [2026-01-29] Verdict: cars like this are "actually ideal" for cold/sleet/pothole
  climates (New Jersey, Michigan, Minnesota) but not for road-tripping; it's his
  favorite-looking Ioniq, especially in snow/slush, and one of his favorite EVs to live
  with right now. (Marques)
