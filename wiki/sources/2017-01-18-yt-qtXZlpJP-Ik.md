# Dope Tech: The Best Drones! (@mkbhd, 2017-01-18)

## Summary
A solo Dope Tech episode built almost entirely around the DJI Mavic Pro, which Marques frames as the current pinnacle of consumer drones — the point where quadcopters, tiny stabilized cameras, gimbals, propellers, sensors, and comms tech converge into a portable, foldable "badass little thousand-dollar toy." He lays out an explicit evaluation rubric for drones (the parallel to his smartphone-completeness checklist): good performance, good controls, and a good camera. He walks through the Mavic's folding portability (fits a backpack, jacket pocket), its speed/stability/4-mile range, the hybrid drone-plus-video controller with the phone clipped in via the DJI app, the removable 3,830 mAh battery giving a ~25-minute flight time that he rates at the long end for drones, and the 4K/30fps gimbal-mounted camera whose smartphone-sized sensor he argues is a non-issue for 99% of uses. The emotional core is the drone as a video/cinematography tool — he enumerates shots impossible any other way (Google-Maps zoom-out, cliff-reveal, cityscape-over-river, top-down, over-water parallax) and the smart autonomous modes (follow mode, mobile follow) that beat manual flying. He explicitly credits collaborator "Dom" for the more advanced shots (quarantined below) and teases future drone work, including new car-video angles and VR head-controlled camera control.

## Key claims
- [2017-01-18] Drones, TVs, and VR headsets were the three things you'd see everywhere if you walked the CES floor long enough; drones have come a long way in just the past couple of years.
- [2017-01-18] The DJI Mavic Pro (released a couple months prior), despite a couple of issues it has had, "pretty much represents the pinnacle of consumer drones" and how far they've come.
- [2017-01-18] His stated criteria for what makes a drone complete/"dope" — the drone analogue of his smartphone staples (good display/battery/camera) — are three things: good performance, good controls, and a good camera.
- [2017-01-18] The Mavic Pro's defining trait is size: thanks to its folding design it fits almost any bag, backpack, travel bag, or even a large jacket pocket, whereas most camera drones (from DJI and others) are big enough to need their own carrying case or box to travel.
- [2017-01-18] Performance is right up there with the best — you never find yourself limited by speed, acceleration, or stability; it works up to four miles away, where the real challenge is just keeping visual orientation on the horizon to fly it back.
- [2017-01-18] The controller is clever because it's part drone controls and part video controls, offloading much of the power to your smartphone: you clip in an Android or iOS phone running the DJI app to get camera recording controls plus live drone information, while the top of the controller is a standard joystick for up/down/lateral/forward/back/turning.
- [2017-01-18] The Mavic has a removable ~3,830 mAh battery — not much bigger than a smartphone's — giving roughly a 25-minute flight time before it wants to return and land; that figure sounds small but is actually near the upper end of drone flight times on a single battery, and batteries recharge fairly quickly, so flying long means carrying a couple of spares to swap.
- [2017-01-18] Battery/performance/controls are all secondary to the main reason he'd buy a drone: putting a camera in the air.
- [2017-01-18] The built-in 4K/30fps gimbal-mounted camera plays to DJI's core strength (stabilization of larger cameras translating well to this gimbal); the sensor is essentially smartphone-sized with a super-wide lens, and given how good smartphone cameras have gotten, that small sensor is "no problem for 99% of uses" — imagine an iPhone 7 or Galaxy S7 Edge camera up in the sky.
- [2017-01-18] This was explicitly not a review — he only messed with flying for a couple of hours, didn't touch focus points or manual mode, and shot in auto — yet it immediately showed its potential as a video tool, delivering shots you can't get with any other camera.
- [2017-01-18] Shots he highlights as unique to drones: the Google-Maps-style zoom-out (a favorite), rising up a cliff to reveal a square mile of city (shot in windy Hoboken, where the Mavic was "pretty rock solid"), the movie-opening cityscape over the river of NYC, top-down shots, and an over-water parallax shot that would normally imply an impossible camera-operator move.
- [2017-01-18] Built-in smart features using contrast-based focusing expand what's possible: follow mode locks the drone in place and rotates it/the camera to follow you hands-free, and mobile follow modes track you through the air — and automated tracking beats manual following, which is much harder than it looks even for someone good with controllers.
- [2017-01-18] Drones as a video tool are the most exciting part to him; he's already thinking about new possibilities like a VR headset that controls the camera by head movement, and new car-video angles never tried before — and this won't be his last drone video.
