# How Google is Taking Back Android! (@mkbhd, 2013-06-07)

## Summary
A sharp structural analysis: Google's quiet, deliberate move of **Android's core apps out
of the OS and into the Play Store** — turning Android into a **"partly rolling release"**
so features/bug-fixes ship without waiting on carriers' OS-update gatekeeping. His clearest
statement of the **update-fragmentation mechanism** and a pointed Verizon critique.

## Key claims
- [2013-06-07] **The rolling-release insight**: "Google has a long, slow, deliberate history
  of pulling core apps out of Android [keyboard, Talk, Calendar, Chrome, music] and shipping
  them through the Play Store… Android is becoming a *partly rolling release* — actual OS
  updates become few, far between, and big, while hundreds of little app updates roll
  alongside." Frames the strategy with the correct software-engineering term.
- [2013-06-07] **Why it matters — the update-fragmentation fix**: "before, a bug in a core
  part like the keyboard needed an over-the-air update of the entire OS to fix — and dealing
  with carriers to push a major OS update to everyone is just about impossible. Decoupling
  the apps makes it way easier to update everyone." The mechanism behind Android's slow
  updates, explained (his recurring fragmentation theme).
- [2013-06-07] **Official-Google-app > third-party replica**: "having the Google version is
  almost always better than a replica — when Google adds a feature, everyone with the Play
  Store version gets it, rather than someone re-coding it for every phone." Reasons about
  maintenance incentives.
- [2013-06-07] **Pointed carrier/Verizon critique**: "Verizon especially sucks at updating
  phones… the Verizon Galaxy Nexus came months late, got bloatware, and Verizon held every
  OTA update hostage until they could force their stuff in — they killed the Nexus
  experience. Google learned its lesson." Names a company's bad behavior directly (even-
  handed toward the ecosystem he favors).
- [2013-06-07] **Credits Apple's tight control as the contrast**: "Apple does a really good
  job getting their new OS out to all their phones because they control it tightly; Android
  is open source, so Google doesn't control it as tightly." Balanced comparison, crediting
  the rival's advantage.
