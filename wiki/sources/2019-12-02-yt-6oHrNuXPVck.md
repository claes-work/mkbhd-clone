# RETRO TECH: POLAROID (@mkbhd, 2019-12-02)

## Summary
An episode of Marques's "Retro Tech" documentary series in which he unboxes and learns to use the Polaroid SX-70 — the oldest piece of tech he says he has ever unboxed, released about 20 years before he was born — and traces the instant camera's history, cultural impact, and decline through a series of guest interviews. Marques narrates and hosts throughout, but the bulk of the historical and technical substance is delivered by outside experts and collaborators: Polaroid historians/authors and curators (identified in captions as Depew, Bonanos, Santiago, Stern), a comedic commentator (Wood), New York instant-photography specialist "Phil V.," and two fellow YouTubers who join him for hands-on segments — photographer Peter McKinnon (a "Dope or Nope" tour of oddball Polaroid models: the 1995 600 Talking Camera, the 1985 JobPro, and a 1973 8x10 camera) and model/coder Karlie Kloss (a Polaroid image-manipulation segment using heat, bleach, and lemon juice). The narrative arc: photography before Polaroid was a slow, lab-based, days-long process; Harvard student Edwin Land invented the polarizing filter (his first product, which built the company), then conceived instant film in 1943 after his young daughter asked why she couldn't see a photo immediately; it took ~30 years for that idea to reach the SX-70, which introduced "integral" self-developing film. Land was a showman whose theatrical shareholder demos are credited as a template later used by Steve Jobs and Apple. Polaroid became ubiquitous by the early '80s, got a publicity boost from OutKast's 2003 "Hey Ya!" ("shake it like a Polaroid picture" — advice the company disowned), but was "flat-footed" for the digital era despite building an early digital camera, and was largely destroyed by the digital revolution before a small-scale analog resurgence. Marques closes that instant photography — the ability to capture and instantly share a memory — will endure "for the rest of humanity."

## Key claims
- [2019-12-02] Marques frames the Polaroid SX-70 as the oldest piece of tech he has ever unboxed, released roughly 20 years before he was born, and credits it with changing how we think about photography forever. (Marques)
- [2019-12-02] Polaroid film photos develop in about 10 to 15 minutes; the SX-70 is a collapsible all-metal camera that Marques found hard to open (had to "yank" it) and initially struggled to eject a photo from, comparing the jam to how printers jam. (Marques, hands-on)
- [2019-12-02] Before Polaroid, photography was a much slower process — photographers shot film and took it to a lab, taking days; long exposure times are why people in old photos aren't smiling. (guests — Depew on the lab process; Wood on the comedic "nobody's smiling" point)
- [2019-12-02] Edwin Land was more chemist than photographer and, as a Harvard student, invented the polarizing filter — a filter that controls the amount of light getting through — which was his first product and built the company (e.g. Cool-Ray Polaroid sunglasses). (guest — Bonanos)
- [2019-12-02] In 1943, on a family vacation, Land photographed his three-year-old daughter, who asked why she couldn't see the picture right away; he reportedly spent that evening working out a rough plan for an instant camera. Instant film was conceived in 1943 but took ~30 years to come to fruition. (guests — Depew, Bonanos)
- [2019-12-02] The SX-70 was the ultimate realization of Land's vision and introduced "integral" film — a sandwich holding the positive, negative, and developing chemistry under a Mylar sheet, with three chemical pods; rollers spread the chemistry and a full dark-room process happens inside the thin sheet, described as a quantum leap in photographic technology. (guests — Depew, Bonanos)
- [2019-12-02] Phil V., described as effectively "the mayor of instant photography in New York City," calls the Polaroid a tool for communication and a conversation starter, and notes the SX-70 uses a glass lens (versus plastic on other Polaroids) that yields a very sharp image when exposure, lighting, and focus are right — no room for error, but a perfect photo if you know what you're doing. (guest — Phil V.)
- [2019-12-02] Edwin Land was a showman who understood the tech was too much to simply explain, so he demoed it at theatrical corporate shareholder meetings; he is largely credited with creating the presentation format later used by Steve Jobs, Apple, and many other companies. (guests — Stern, Depew)
- [2019-12-02] The first-generation SX-70 was expensive — roughly a thousand dollars in today's money with inflation, with film around $40 a pack (~$4–6 per photo) — yet couldn't be made fast enough to meet demand and was the hot tech item of its year; by the early '80s the basic Polaroid was as familiar as a household phone. (guests — Wood on pricing; Bonanos on demand/ubiquity)
- [2019-12-02] Marques notes the SX-70 was an icon but not Polaroid's only camera — the company had ~70 years of instant-camera innovation. In a "Dope or Nope" segment with Peter McKinnon they review the 1995 Polaroid 600 Talking Camera (records/plays pre-set and custom audio prompts; judged "dope"), the 1985 JobPro made for construction sites (survives drop tests but judged "nope" — no more rugged than a normal Polaroid), and a 1973 8x10 large-format instant camera (requires presetting focus/exposure, higher resolution than expected; judged "dope"). (Marques + guest McKinnon)
- [2019-12-02] OutKast's 2003 hit "Hey Ya!" brought enormous attention to Polaroid, though the lyric "shake it like a Polaroid picture" is bad advice — you shouldn't shake an SX-70 photo, as it can crack the image. (guest commentary — Santiago/panel)
- [2019-12-02] Polaroid actually built one of the first digital cameras but didn't pursue digital seriously because it was making huge money on film; it was "flat-footed" and largely destroyed by the digital revolution, with a small-scale analog resurgence today — including adopting the square image on Instagram and inspiring filters that emulate analog effects like light leaks. (guests — Bonanos, Stern, Depew)
- [2019-12-02] In a hands-on manipulation segment, Marques and Karlie Kloss establish that Polaroid development is heat-based (applying heat accelerates the chemical reaction/development), while bleach and lemon juice (acidic) alter the developed image — the bleach having the stronger effect — demonstrating that "what you see is what you get" can still be creatively disrupted. (Marques + guest Kloss — mixed)
- [2019-12-02] Legacy framing: Land devoted his life to letting people see photos instantly; that instant image carries over into smartphones (every digital image is "instant," and Polaroid was the first to do it), and the act of handing someone a physical instant photo is a "gift" that prefigures the digital idea of "share." (guests — Depew, Bonanos, Santiago, Stern)
