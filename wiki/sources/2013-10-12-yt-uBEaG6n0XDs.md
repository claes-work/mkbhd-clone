# Apple iPhone 5s Review! (@mkbhd, 2013-10-12)

## Summary
Marques's first-ever iPhone review, framed deliberately from the perspective of a heavy Google-services user and someone who normally carries large Android phones (Galaxy Note 3, Moto X). Reviewing the iPhone 5s in space gray after living with it longer than a typical review window, he calls it a beautifully crafted, extremely compact, reliable device let down for him mainly by its small 4-inch display, so-so in-use battery life, and iOS 7's locked-down, uncustomizable functionality. He notes the 5s ships specs that on paper resemble a 2011 flagship (Galaxy S2) yet performs on par with modern 8-core Android phones because Apple controls hardware and software end to end. Highlights are Touch ID, a fast and low-light-capable 8MP camera with 120fps slow-mo, and the new 64-bit A7 chip; gripes are the half-second unlock animation, single-driver "fake stereo" bottom speaker, and iOS's take-it-or-leave-it design.

## Key claims
- [2013-10-12] This is Marques's first iPhone review; it took him roughly seven iPhone generations to finally review one, and he approaches it as a heavy Google-services user (Gmail, Drive, Calendar, tasks).
- [2013-10-12] The 5s comes in three colors; he picked space gray as his favorite. It runs iOS 7 with the familiar iPhone icon grid.
- [2013-10-12] The 4-inch display is the smallest on any current flagship, making the phone one of the most compact mobile devices, period — light and reachable one-handed, which Apple is proud of, though he argues a slightly larger 4.5-inch display would still allow one-handed corner reach.
- [2013-10-12] It is one of the most fragile-feeling phones despite not actually being fragile: extremely well built, premium metal like the HTC One, tightly assembled with no creaking or twisting, but the flat lack of curves plus the glass back (like the Nexus 4) make him feel he shouldn't drop it.
- [2013-10-12] Battery is 1570 mAh — roughly half the Note 3's 3000+ mAh; the entire iPhone is about the size of a Note 3's battery. Pro: it charges very quickly. Con: in-use battery life is shorter than larger-battery phones and about 5% worse than the iPhone 5.
- [2013-10-12] Standby is excellent — an iPhone left overnight off-charge drops only from 100% to ~97%, where a similar-size-battery Android might wake at ~80%; iOS manages background apps well during sleep.
- [2013-10-12] Hardware craftsmanship is high: solid aluminum sides/back, precisely crafted non-wobbling buttons with laser-etched volume markings, chamfered edges angled slightly inward, precisely milled speaker grille holes; the small package makes the detail hard to fully appreciate, so the HTC One still feels just as good in hand.
- [2013-10-12] The bottom "stereo" speaker grilles are cosmetic — it's actually a single driver on the right that echoes to the left; covering the left changes nothing, covering the right mutes it entirely.
- [2013-10-12] Spec-sheet comparison: the iPhone 5s (dual-core ~1.3GHz, 1GB RAM, 32GB, 8MP camera, 1570 mAh) mirrors the April-2011 Samsung Galaxy S2 (dual-core 1.2GHz, 1GB RAM, 32GB, 8MP, 1650 mAh), yet performs on par with 2013 flagships that have 8-core chips, double the RAM, and 20MP cameras — because Apple controls and optimizes the whole hardware/software stack.
- [2013-10-12] iOS 7 is a visual overhaul — "a layer of gloss" — with functionality essentially identical to iOS 6; you can't customize icons, keyboard, lock screen, or dialer ("you'll probably like it or learn to like it").
- [2013-10-12] His biggest iOS 7 gripe is the unlock animation, which takes a solid half second during which the home screen is unresponsive to touch; he wishes he could speed it up. He also flags visual fragmentation where apps not updated for iOS 7 fall back to the old keyboard.
- [2013-10-12] iOS remains a great choice for many — 10 million sold the first weekend — and as a Google-services user he can get most core apps from the App Store, so the situation "is really not that bad at all."
- [2013-10-12] Touch ID is the fingerprint sensor in the home button: unlocks the phone and authenticates iTunes purchases, registers up to 10 fingers, works accurately most of the time; caveat — after every restart you must re-enter your full iTunes password before Touch ID works in the App Store again.
- [2013-10-12] Camera framework: quality and control each scored ~8/10, striking a likable balance (unlike a Lumia 1020 at 10/10 quality but hard for a stranger to use). Fast in multiple senses — fast capture/processing, fast HDR, fast/wide aperture (~f/2.0–2.2, slightly softer than iPhone 5 but great for low light), and fast 120fps slow-motion video slowed to 30fps.
- [2013-10-12] Low-light shooting was what he most enjoyed — it "can essentially see in the dark"; photos are accurate, saturated, detailed, only slightly softer than the iPhone 5 due to the wider aperture; among the best smartphone cameras available.
- [2013-10-12] Verdict: great performance, smooth animations, super-compact form factor, excellent build/materials, plus future-proofing from the 64-bit architecture and Apple's ecosystem control; a very reliable, familiar device that always behaves predictably. Not worth rushing to upgrade from an iPhone 5, but a great choice coming from older iPhones. The display (loves the quality, wishes it were bigger) and the slightly-too-small, fragile-feeling build are the most polarizing points; he closes by noting he's "not a big iOS user."
