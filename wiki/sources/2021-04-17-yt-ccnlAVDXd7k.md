# Retro Tech: Smart Homes (@mkbhd, 2021-04-17)

## Summary
A Retro Tech (Season 2) documentary tracing the evolution of home-automation tech from
the 1950s "time-saving devices" and the TV remote control through 1980s power-line
control (X-10), early voice-activated hubs, and on to today's WiFi-connected smart home —
ending on the future framed as energy efficiency and climate. Marques hosts: he unboxes a
1992 **"Smart Clapper"** ("clap on, clap off"), runs a "Dope or Nope" early-remotes
segment with comedian **Dulcé Sloan** (a ~20-year-old voice-activated "inVoca" remote —
nope; a 1995 remote-control locator — dope), and a hands-on X-10 / early-smart-home test
with fellow YouTuber **Sam Sheffer** (Radio Shack "Plug 'n Power" and the 1986 "Master
Voice Butler-In-A-Box," the first voice-activated smart-home hub). Historians/commentators
(Erica, Sam, Anneke, Lorraine, Scott) narrate the cultural arc, including the sci-fi
influence (2001, Back to the Future II, Iron Man's JARVIS, Star Trek's replicator) and
Steve Wozniak's post-Apple universal-remote venture (CL 9) that failed for being too
complicated. The episode closes with an on-camera interview with **Bill Gates** —
positioned as environmentalist/philanthropist — on smart homes, energy monitoring, heat
pumps, and the challenge of mass adoption. Because this is a heavily-interviewed
documentary, ONLY Marques-attributed narration/reactions are persona/voice data; all guest
statements (especially Bill Gates) are quarantined below as CONTEXT and excluded from
persona training.

## Key claims
- [2021-04-17] (Marques) Retro Tech "rewinds the clock to look at the tech of the past that
  we thought would be our future"; today's category is smart-home technology.
- [2021-04-17] (Marques) The 1992 "Smart Clapper" is a smart outlet that listens for claps —
  "no software, no programming, no apps"; he frames it as the iconic origin of the
  clap-to-toggle idea.
- [2021-04-17] (Marques) On the 1986 Master Voice "Butler-In-A-Box" — the first
  voice-activated smart-home hub, connectable to a landline — he draws "a straight line to
  what we have today, and what we're looking forward to in the future… This is what we
  want. An assistant," while conceding the live demo (repeated "Please repeat… Call Mom")
  was the classic "bleeding-edge tech… not ready yet" experience.
- [2021-04-17] (Marques) He frames the internet/WiFi era as the real inflection: "instead
  of just having smart devices, you had smart devices that could talk to each other" — the
  home itself becomes smart (thermostats, doorbell cameras, "watching our cats").
- [2021-04-17] (Marques) Dope-or-Nope verdicts with Dulcé Sloan: the ~2000s "inVoca" voice
  remote ("used in interplanetary space probes") — **nope** (too much work, barely better
  than a button, "that'll make you get divorced"); the 1995 remote-control locator — **dope**
  ("it makes your house just a little bit smarter, no matter how simple it really is").
- [2021-04-17] (Marques) Closing thesis: the future sweet spot is "somewhere in between the
  Clapper and its simplicity and ease of use and convenience, and the Nest, Sense, smart
  windows of the world" — and smart homes may be closer to the sci-fi vision than any other
  category Retro Tech has covered.
- [2021-04-17] (Erica/Anneke/Lorraine/Sam — CONTEXT, not persona data) Smart-home tech
  traces to 1950s–60s suburban "time-saving devices"; the TV remote control was the seed of
  the whole idea; early tech "suffered from this idea that just because it was impressive or
  complicated-looking, then it was good."
- [2021-04-17] (Commentator — CONTEXT) Steve Wozniak believed in remote control so much he
  left Apple to found a company (CL 9) solely to build a better universal remote; it was too
  complicated and ultimately failed.
- [2021-04-17] (Commentator — CONTEXT) The 1970s X-10 system ran automation over a home's
  existing electrical wiring via a control panel — a physical-home "network" before WiFi,
  but limited to on/off of lights and simple appliances.
- [2021-04-17] (Bill Gates — CONTEXT, not persona data) His 1995 book *The Road Ahead*
  discussed home automation; the "digital ability to control those things and to understand
  where you're using electricity… still hasn't happened" in a simple, cheap form.
- [2021-04-17] (Bill Gates — CONTEXT) For most houses the big electricity use is heating and
  cooling; a **heat pump** is "fairly cheap" and some houses can switch off natural gas
  (which has carbon emissions) to electricity and actually save money.
- [2021-04-17] (Bill Gates — CONTEXT) Energy-smart homes tend to get pioneered in new
  developments marketing themselves as "city of the future"; the real challenge is mass
  adoption — "more than just a few."
