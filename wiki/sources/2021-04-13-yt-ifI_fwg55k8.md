# Retro Tech: Flying Cars (@mkbhd, 2021-04-13)

## Summary
A Retro Tech (Season 2) documentary episode on the flying-car dream and its real-world descendants. Marques narrates and hosts: unboxing the original **Segway** (the famously over-hyped "Ginger" that Steve Jobs said would be "bigger than the PC" and turned out to be a scooter), then tracing the flying-car fantasy through sci-fi (The Jetsons, Back to the Future, Blade Runner) and real attempts (the 1940s Aerocar). A physics segment with car-tech enthusiast **Gali Russell (HyperChange)** puts hard numbers on why cars don't fly (~1.6 megawatts to hover a ~5,000-lb car; a 100kWh battery would sustain hover only 3–4 minutes vs. ~400 miles on the ground). A "Dope or Nope" comedy segment with **Michael Ian Black** tests hoverboards, an Airwheel, and the Segway Loomo robot. It closes with hoverboard inventors **Alexandru Duru & Philippe Maalouf (Omni Hoverboards)** and Marques's reflection that flying cars are probably a bad idea but recreational individual flight is still worth chasing. Transcript is professionally captioned with reliable speaker labels.

## Key claims
- [2021-04-13] The flying car recurs as the cultural marker for "the future has arrived"; American obsession is tied to car culture and "that notion of freedom." (Negin & Tim, interviewees — historians/commentators)
- [2021-04-13] Real attempts existed — the 1940s **Aerocar** had detachable wings and a 60-mph road mode that could take off — but "cooler heads prevailed" and it didn't reach production. (Tim & Joseph, interviewees)
- [2021-04-13] Sci-fi (The Jetsons, Star Wars, Blade Runner, Back to the Future) shaped the expectation, but "envisioning a piece of technology isn't enough to bring it into being — the physics just doesn't seem to want to play along." (Marques, narration)
- [2021-04-13] Physics of a flying car (with Gali Russell): it's "a first principles physics problem of literally moving all of this air super quickly with enough force to get some sort of lift-off"; ~1.6 MW just to hover a ~5,000-lb car; two approaches — a giant-drone propeller system, or Tesla/Elon's repurposed cold-gas rocket thrusters — either of which would consume all the interior space. A 100kWh battery = 3–4 min of hover vs. ~400 mi on the road, and only counting the driver's weight. (Gali Russell, interviewee, + Marques narration for the calc summary)
- [2021-04-13] Marques's own take: growing up he "didn't think they should fly" — he wanted cars to be "more like the high-tech stuff we have today"; the Tesla "in some way embodies that ... it has software, it can kind of drive itself." (Marques)
- [2021-04-13] The Segway backstory: inventor Dean Kamen's "Ginger"/"IT," hyped by Steve Jobs ("bigger than the PC") and rumored "bigger than the internet," revealed on Good Morning America as a scooter — "there was something about the Segway that just evaporated people's dignity." Remove the handlebars and it became the hoverboard craze — which "caught fire all the time" (SNL parody). (Tim, Negin, Joseph, interviewees)
- [2021-04-13] Dope or Nope verdicts (with Michael Ian Black): the self-balancing hoverboard — both give it a **dope**; the Airwheel — **nope** ("a great solution to a problem nobody had"); the Segway **Loomo** follow-robot — **nope** (cool tech, no good use, not cheap). (Marques & Michael Ian Black)
- [2021-04-13] The **Omni Hoverboard** (Alexandru Duru & Philippe Maalouf): a real functioning hoverboard using an array of 10 miniature propellers + battery bank + handheld throttle, able to rise as high as 100 meters. Their design choice: prioritize functionality over looking like the Back to the Future board — "It's not like you're riding the vehicle. It's you yourself that's flying ... it's like a surfboard, but you're surfing the air ... a bit like you acquired a superpower." (Alexandru Duru & Philippe Maalouf, interviewees)
- [2021-04-13] Marques's closing thesis: flying cars ("giving everyone helicopters") is "probably not the best idea," but "I still love the idea of recreational individual flight ... if you ask anyone what superpower they would want, a lot of people would say they would just want to be able to fly like a bird. So maybe let's keep working on that." (Marques)
