# New Myspace Design: Explained! (@mkbhd, 2012-09-25)

## Summary
A short look at Myspace's 2012 (Justin Timberlake–backed) redesign. The one genuinely useful idea is a
crisp distinction he draws: **Myspace is now a good *website* but not a good *social network*** — the
design is excellent, but a network's value is its people, and almost no one is there. A neat, dated
separation of design quality from network value.

## Key claims
- [2012-09-25] **The website-vs-network distinction — the takeaway**: "Myspace is now actually a good
  website. It's not a good *social network* anymore, because no one really uses it — but it's a good
  website." He praises the redesign's craft while denying it fixes the fundamental problem (no users).
- [2012-09-25] **Design credited specifically and generously**: a horizontally-scrolling timeline/photo
  feed with the notification bar and navigation moved to the bottom, a "Metro UI look" reminiscent of the
  new Outlook.com — "the layout, the general design, the fonts, the styles… they actually look good.
  Thumbs up to them, the designers did a great job." Craft acknowledged independent of the product's
  viability.
- [2012-09-25] **Names the surviving use case honestly**: "video and music are a really big part of
  Myspace right now — a lot of smaller artists and bands host their music on Myspace," and the redesign
  puts media "very center stage." He identifies who still uses it and why, rather than declaring it dead.
- [2012-09-25] **Won't overclaim his own reaction**: "this particular change might not bring me back to
  Myspace… no, I'm just kidding, I won't see you over there." Honest that a good redesign doesn't
  overcome the network effect for him personally.
- [2012-09-25] Small dated detail: it lets you **sign in with Facebook**, and gates access behind a
  "request an invite" flow — which he gently mocks ("as if you have to wait in line to get into
  Myspace").
