# Dope Tech: The 4K OLED Wallpaper TV! (@mkbhd, 2017-05-01)

## Summary
A solo Dope Tech episode on the LG Signature OLED W7 — the 65-inch "Wallpaper" 4K OLED TV — which Marques has in the studio courtesy of LG. Unlike the show-floor concept TVs that never ship, this one is real and buyable ($8,000). His fascination is the industrial-design feat: the panel is officially 3.8mm thick, thinner than the thinnest phone LG makes, about as thick as two SD cards, and it hangs on the wall like a picture frame — hooked onto a thin top rail and held flush by magnets down the middle and bottom, with no stand (wall-mount only). The trick that makes it possible is that all the electronics, ports, and speakers live in the accompanying Dolby-Atmos soundbar, which connects to the panel via a single flat ribbon cable carrying power and signal. He rates the OLED itself excellent (1,000 nits, pitch-black blacks, 99% DCI-P3, Dolby Vision / HDR10 / HLG / Technicolor HDR), and the soundbar loud and clean with a convincing reflected-surround effect. His "why it's dope tech" thesis: it's not a new concept (offloading electronics to the base has been done), but taking away as much as physically possible produces a super-immersive experience where you stop noticing it's a TV — it looks like an image pasted onto the wall. Practical caveat: how "dope" your setup looks depends entirely on how well you hide that cable.

## Key claims
- [2017-05-01] The LG Signature OLED W7 is real and on sale, unlike most of the coolest concept TVs shown at trade shows that never go on sale and are just showcases.
- [2017-05-01] The "W" stands for "wallpaper," a fitting name because the TV is so thin it literally looks like a cutout or a wallpaper applied to a 65-inch section of wall.
- [2017-05-01] The panel is officially 3.8mm thick total (including the wall mount); there is no stand and no way to keep it upright — you must wall-mount it.
- [2017-05-01] The OLED module is essentially a 65-inch sheet of pixels that hooks onto a tiny top wall-mount like a picture frame and uses magnets through the middle and bottom to stay flush with the wall.
- [2017-05-01] The panel is thinner than many household objects normally considered thin — about the thickness of two SD cards, roughly a key held against the wall — and thinner than the thinnest phone LG makes.
- [2017-05-01] The OLED is excellent quality (as it should be for the price): up to 1,000 nits (extremely bright), pitch-black blacks, 99% of DCI color space, and it supports Dolby Vision, HDR10, HLG and Technicolor HDR — covering essentially any format.
- [2017-05-01] It's a 4K panel, so detail, contrast and sharpness are incredible even up close.
- [2017-05-01] All the "guts" — ports, speakers, and remaining components — live in the soundbar, which is an equally important part of the setup as the OLED; everything connects through one flat ribbon cable out the bottom of the panel that carries both signal and power.
- [2017-05-01] The soundbar has four HDMI ports, a couple of USB, Ethernet, etc.; for maximum "visual flotation" you'd ideally route the cable into the wall (needs professional install and fire-code shielding), but double-sided tape works fine and the cable isn't that noticeable once you're watching.
- [2017-05-01] The soundbar sounds great — loud without much distortion — with speakers facing front, sideways and straight up, throwing sound off the walls to create a convincing surround-sound effect.
- [2017-05-01] The reason the panel can be so thin is that on the wall it's literally just the OLED, some shielding, and a few magnets; the dopeness of the setup depends mostly on how well you can hide the cable.
- [2017-05-01] After a couple of days he still sometimes wonders why it had to get so thin — the difference between a 1-inch TV and a tenth-of-an-inch panel — but using it, the thinness plus very thin bezels create a super-immersive viewing and gaming experience.
- [2017-05-01] Bottom line: at $8,000 most people won't buy it; it's more a showcase of what the bleeding edge of tech can look like.
- [2017-05-01] The thin-panel-plus-electronics-in-the-base concept isn't new (LG and Samsung have done it before), but this takes it to the next level; that's why it's dope tech — seeing what's possible by removing as much as possible until you get an experience where it just looks like an image copied and pasted onto a wall.
