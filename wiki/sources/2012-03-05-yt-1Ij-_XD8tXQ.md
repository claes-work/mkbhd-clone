# Asus Zenbook UX31 Review! (@mkbhd, 2012-03-05)

## Summary
A review of the Asus Zenbook UX31, opened by calling it "a picture-perfect definition of an ultrabook"
— which sets up the category explainer four days later. Standard walkthrough of ports, hinge, build,
keyboard, display and internals, and then the part worth keeping: **an unqualified negative on the
trackpad**, delivered with a mid-sentence reversal, an argument for why it matters more on this class
of machine than any other, and an explicit acknowledgment that the fault might be his unit or his own
preference.

## Key claims
- [2012-03-05] **The negative, delivered without cushioning**: "and the trackpad… **no, it's just bad.**
  I mean, it's big and smooth and has nice hidden buttons and everything, but for some reason mine just
  wasn't that sensitive at all."
- [2012-03-05] **Why the flaw is disqualifying for the category** — the argument, not just the
  complaint: "if you're getting an ultra-portable laptop you don't really want to carry around a
  secondary mouse — so the keyboard and the trackpad should be really top-notch. But this trackpad let
  me down."
- [2012-03-05] **Two candidate explanations offered against his own verdict**: "I don't know if it was
  just mine, or maybe I'm a mouse and keyboard kind of guy," plus "I feel like it could use a firmware
  update or something." He states the fault and its possible non-generality in the same breath.
- [2012-03-05] **Build and design praised specifically, including an absence**: "there's no ports on the
  back of this laptop either, which is perfect — the way it should be"; a spun-metal finish that
  "reminds me of the Transformer Prime"; and a hinge engineered well enough to open one-handed —
  "I can tell that Asus spent time, designed it properly, put the right parts in the right places just so
  that it could do this. Does your laptop do that? I didn't think so."
- [2012-03-05] **A stated aesthetic preference overridden by execution**: "I might not be a fan of glossy
  on the desktop, but they pulled it off here" — 13.3" 1600×900 IPS, decent viewing angles, and bright
  enough for outdoor use.
- [2012-03-05] **Credits the component he thinks is actually responsible**: performance held up while
  editing video on battery, but "I'm convinced that the **128 gig SSD** in this laptop had a lot to do
  with that" — the same SSD-is-the-real-difference conclusion he reached in the Mac Mini finale three
  days earlier ([[2012-03-02-yt-G_H6mRHS9pc]]).
- [2012-03-05] **An unexpected strength flagged for the industry**: the speakers "were really loud, it
  surprised me — kind of blew me away actually. I'd like to see more of the technology they used in these
  speakers in more mobile devices."
- [2012-03-05] Specs recorded: Windows 7 Home Premium, Intel Core i5-2557M ULV at 1.7 GHz, USB 3.0 plus
  a second USB, mini VGA, mini HDMI, card reader, non-removable battery, 17 mm thick.
- [2012-03-05] Keyboard assessed relatively, in the disclosed-baseline style: "it's all right, it's not
  the best I've ever used but it's definitely not the worst… the size was fine for even my
  mammoth-sized hands."

## Notes

- ★ L3-candidate (method): **a negative verdict paired with its own possible defeaters.** He doesn't
  soften the judgement ("it's just bad") but immediately supplies the two reasons a viewer might discount
  it — a defective unit, or his own mouse-and-keyboard preference. This is the disclosed-baseline
  discipline applied to a *criticism* rather than a score, and it is the cleanest such instance so far.
- ★ L3-candidate (method, small): **weighting a flaw by device class** — a mediocre trackpad is a minor

  the workaround. Judging a component against the product's purpose rather than in isolation.
