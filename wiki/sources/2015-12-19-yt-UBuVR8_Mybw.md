# Smartphone Awards: 2015! (@mkbhd, 2015-12-19)

## Summary
Marques's annual Smartphone Awards for 2015 — a solo "one-stop shop" video framed as a buyer's guide to help viewers "confidently sign that contract" on a phone. He walks through a set of categories, giving a first-place winner, a second-place runner-up, and one or two honorable mentions in each, before crowning an overall phone of the year (MVP). Categories: best big phone, best compact phone, best camera, best budget phone, best battery life (a new category this year), most improved company, best design, "bust of the year," and MVP. The through-line is that 2015 was a strong, competitive year across the board — cameras and budget phones especially "heated up" — and that a buyer would be hard-pressed to go wrong. His overall pick is the Nexus 6P, which he says reflects his own preference for a clean build of Android.

## Key claims
- [2015-12-19] Best big phone — winner: Samsung Galaxy Note 5 (5.7" Quad HD Super AMOLED, big battery/specs, software features that use the screen; framed as an advantage of a software skin over stock Android). Second place: LG V10 (5.7" IPS, arrived late in the year, "showed up LG's own G4," similar productivity features minus the stylus). Honorable mentions: Nexus 6P and Samsung Galaxy S6 Edge Plus (both over 5.5", both premium).
- [2015-12-19] Best compact phone — winner: iPhone 6s (4.7"), because it is "a true full no-compromises flagship" with Apple's latest innovations and highest-end specs; the A9 chip's "ridiculously fast performance" is the standout. Second place (very close): Sony Xperia Z5 Compact, for bringing Z5/Z5 Premium high-end features into a much smaller package. He notes truly high-end compact phones are becoming a "rare breed" as flagships grow.
- [2015-12-19] Best camera — winner: Samsung Galaxy Note 5, "the best point-and-shoot camera on a smartphone right now by a hair" — fast customizable camera app, razor-sharp 16-megapixel photos with punchy color and solid dynamic range, good 4K video; same optics in the Galaxy S6 / S6 Edge / S6 Edge Plus, which he lumps into the win. Second place (very close): LG V10, image quality on par with the Note 5 and the best choice for shooting a lot of smartphone video thanks to its manual video mode. Honorable mentions: iPhone 6s Plus and Nexus 6P (fast, reliable cameras). He stresses the margin between the 1st and 5th best cameras is "not that big."
- [2015-12-19] Best budget phone — winner: OnePlus X ($250 off-contract, no invite; 5" 1080p AMOLED with deep blacks/vivid colors, metal rails, expandable storage, custom OS). Second place: Moto X Pure/Style (2015, just shy of $400 off-contract) — called "one of the more important phones of the year" for setting the precedent that flagships need not be super expensive (legit high-end specs, 21-megapixel camera, near-stock Android); a "spark plug" for later budget-focused phones. Honorable mentions: OnePlus 2 ($389, a daily driver for him) and Nexus 5X ($329, pure stock Android with the Nexus 6P's camera optics). He notes "budget" is hard to define — sub-$400 vs sub-$300.
- [2015-12-19] Best battery life (a NEW category for 2015) — winner: Motorola Droid Turbo 2 ("Droid Max 2" in captions), 3,630 mAh, Moto claims ~2 days, a "legit all-day smartphone" with 1080p display and near-stock Android; aimed at heavy users wanting 5.5 hours of screen-on time or doing navigation/Periscope. Second place: Nexus 6P, which after recent Android Marshmallow (Android M) updates gives him "near 5 hours of screen-on time," some of the best he's had on a high-end phone. Shout-out to LG for two high-end phones with removable batteries — LG V10 and LG G4 (removable battery = fastest form of quick charge). He explicitly added this category because he sees battery life "neglected in the highest-end flagships in favor of stuff like thinness."
- [2015-12-19] Most improved (company) — winner: BlackBerry, via the BlackBerry Priv, "by far the best BlackBerry ever and the most competitive one in a long time"; still expensive and not for everyone (its version of Android with heavy customizations, slider keyboard), but refreshing to see BlackBerry get better "in a weirdly throwback way."
- [2015-12-19] Best design — winner: Samsung Galaxy S6 Edge Plus, singled out in "a sea of black slab phones" for its sloping edges and the display melting over into the metal rails ("dope"); he hopes Samsung makes another Edge phone next year. Runners-up (for standout displays): Motorola Droid Turbo 2 (shatterproof display he can vouch survives real drops) and Sony Xperia Z5 Premium (full 4K display; "super early for these" but looks dope). He notes last year this category was "craziest design"; this year he reframed it as best design.
- [2015-12-19] Bust of the year — HTC One A9. A category he "hates to give but always will." Beyond the striking resemblance to a "certain Apple flagship," as a successor to the HTC One M9 it is disappointing: it sacrificed 4K video capture for a certain chip and dropped the legendary BoomSound speakers for a home button. "Easily the biggest letdown of the year."
- [2015-12-19] MVP / phone of the year — winner: Nexus 6P. He says his heavy use of it hints at his preference for a cleaner build of Android — "as clean as it gets" — with awesome display, battery, camera, performance, and build quality; "a complete package from top to bottom" that "competes in every category" and isn't even the most expensive on the list. Second place: Galaxy Note 5 (he "could easily [have] given it to" the S6 Edge Plus, but the feel in the hand of the Note 5 won him over); he'd give the Note 5 best display and best camera on any smartphone, though he's less a fan of the software and calls it a fingerprint magnet and one of the slipperiest phones ever.
- [2015-12-19] MVP honorable mentions: Moto X (2015) for setting the precedent of what's possible on a budget and driving more quality budget phones and competitive flagship pricing; iPhone 6s / 6s Plus as "the fastest phones I've used this year" (iOS "just flies" on the A9); and Microsoft Lumia 950 XL as "by far the best Windows Phone choice of this year."
- [2015-12-19] Framing/methodology: for each category he gives a first place ("best overall option"), a second place runner-up, and an honorable mention or two. He repeatedly flags picks as "very subjective" and points to full reviews in the description for the phones discussed.
