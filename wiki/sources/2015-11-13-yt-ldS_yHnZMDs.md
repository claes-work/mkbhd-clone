# Smartphone Camera Quality: Explained! (@mkbhd, 2015-11-13)

## Summary
An **"Explained!"** that is the **canonical statement of how he evaluates smartphone cameras** — the method
behind the camera sections of his reviews (and the future Blind Camera Test). He walks the real hierarchy:
**the sensor matters most (bigger > smaller, all else equal — megapixels aren't everything)**, then
aperture, then **OIS (hardware) beats EIS (software)**, and finally **processing** (where phones differ:
sharpening, noise, warm-vs-cool color, saturation, exposure). The closing thesis: image quality is
**subjective** — accurate ≠ most-pleasing — and **"you can't tell how good a camera is by the numbers, like
you can't tell how a car drives by the specs — at the end of the day it's you who decides, by how much you
like the picture."**

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated)
- [2015-11-13] **The sensor is the most important hardware — megapixels aren't everything.** "When all
  other things are held constant, a larger sensor can outperform a smaller one." Fewer/larger pixels help
  low light (HTC's UltraPixel); 12–18 MP gives detail but suffers more in low light. "It's hard to tell a
  lot about a sensor just by the name or the numbers."
- [2015-11-13] **Aperture (lower f = more light + shallow DoF).** Fixed-aperture lenses; "the bigger the
  better — the best phone cameras now have f/2.0, sometimes f/1.9 or f/1.8."
- [2015-11-13] **OIS (hardware) beats EIS (software).** EIS corrects minor handshake but can cause a jello
  effect in video; **OIS is "awesome pretty much all the time"** — better for low light / longer exposures /
  much smoother video. "Not every phone has it, but when it's possible, it's preferred."
- [2015-11-13] **Ignore the vanity specs.** Lens-element count and flash design "don't really tell you much"
  ("I avoid using a flash on smartphone pictures anyway").
- [2015-11-13] **Processing is where phones differ — and it's subjective.** Different sharpening, noise
  reduction, color temperature, saturation, and exposure. "One phone takes an objectively more accurate
  photo; another a less-accurate but more-pleasing one." Cites **Casey Neistat's blind test** (the Nexus
  6P's cooler/brighter, arguably overexposed shot won the popular vote over the more-accurate iPhone 6s).
- [2015-11-13] **The car-specs analogy (the closing thesis).** "It's hard to tell how good a smartphone
  camera is just by the numbers — like it's hard to tell how a car will drive by the specs on paper; you
  don't know until you drive it. A reviewer can tell you how accurate the photos are and the processing
  tendencies (which a software update can change) — but at the end of the day it's **you** who decides how
  good a camera is, based on how much you like the picture."
