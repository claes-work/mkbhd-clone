# Dope Tech of CES 2018! [Part 2] (@mkbhd, 2018-01-14)

## Summary
The second of a two-part Dope Tech set from the CES 2018 show floor in Las Vegas (Part 1 is referenced and linked in-video). This follow-up is crowd-sourced — Marques went back to the floor with Twitter/Reddit suggestions to chase the "more newsworthy" things people wanted to know about. He opens with two things he *couldn't* see in person (Synaptics' under-display fingerprint reader, which he expects in smartphones "as early as this year," and LG's rollable OLED TV, "probably the most hype thing of all of CES"), then runs through what he did see: envelope-pushing TVs (Samsung's 146-inch modular display, Sony's 85-inch 8K at 10,000 nits), the Byton concept EV as a case study in over-hyping dateless/priceless "concept" hardware, Matias's buyable Space-Gray-style wireless/backlit keyboards, a Mark Roberts Motion Control Bolt camera robot, Panasonic's Tesla-in-the-booth (useful during CES's actual power outage), a floor full of robots including an AI ping-pong robot that scales to your skill, and phone hype (Huawei Mate 10 Pro vs RED Hydrogen One). The through-line is his recurring trade-show skepticism: separate the real, buyable "dope tech" from concept-car vaporware, and wait to see what actually comes to fruition.

## Key claims
- [2018-01-14] This is Part 2 of a two-part CES 2018 Dope Tech set, deliberately built from viewer suggestions (Twitter/Reddit) to cover the "more newsworthy" items people asked about after Part 1.
- [2018-01-14] Synaptics was demoing an under-display-glass fingerprint reader; he didn't get to try it, but he thinks it will have "pretty sweet implications in smartphones as early as this year."
- [2018-01-14] LG's rollable OLED TV was, in his view, "probably the most hype thing of all of CES" that year — a flexible motorized panel that rolls into a box, can partial-roll to a 21:9 ultra-wide ratio for bar-free movies, or roll almost fully down to a thin weather/info UI strip. He couldn't see it in person (pulled from the booth quickly) but heard it may actually ship "sooner than we think."
- [2018-01-14] CES is really "cars and TVs" — he jokes they could rename it "the cars and electronics show and nobody would even bat an eye."
- [2018-01-14] The dopest TVs were pushing resolution and size: a 45-inch OLED is trivial now, so the standouts were Samsung's 146-inch modular display and Sony's 85-inch 8K HDR set blasting 10,000 nits of peak brightness — impressive, and he hopes such tech shows up in cheaper real products later.
- [2018-01-14] The Byton EV was "probably the number one most hyped new electric car at CES" and checked every concept-car cliché: one working prototype, a huge (potentially distracting) dashboard-replacing display, no door handles, cameras instead of side mirrors, and angling for a Best-of-CES award "without announcing a price or a release date."
- [2018-01-14] He explicitly walks back his own past behavior — he says he'd now avoid giving concept cars "too much hype like I have in the past," because the key word is *concept*: "a physical representation of an idea," not a real product, with no release date and nothing you can buy yet.
- [2018-01-14] What you *can* buy right now and he rates as dope tech: Matias keyboards — essentially doing what people wanted Apple to do, selling a Space-Gray-style wireless keyboard (plus other colors) that anyone can buy without an iMac Pro, including wired and wireless backlit versions (wireless = white on/off only; wired has a physical wheel on the back to cycle colors).
- [2018-01-14] He saw a cinema motion-controlled camera robot — the Mark Roberts Motion Control Bolt and Bolt Jr — a programmable robot arm that executes fast, ultra-steady camera moves "human hands could never do," and admits it gave him "all kinds of ideas for tech videos."
- [2018-01-14] Panasonic kept its tradition of parking a Tesla in its booth — which he notes was "probably pretty useful during that power outage": CES, the biggest electronics show in the world, actually lost power.
- [2018-01-14] Robots were everywhere (cleaning, companionship, Scrabble, dancing). He played the AI ping-pong robot and found it "uncanny" — it rates your play (spin/speed) via a number projected on the net and scales its own difficulty to match you; he says he got to about "a 93 out of a hundred" and quit once he took the lead.
- [2018-01-14] The Huawei Mate 10 Pro "may have overtaken red hydrogen one as the most hyped phone of 2018," at least on his timeline, and he teases a "review slash rant" about it.
- [2018-01-14] Closing trade-show thesis: the wait is now on to see which CES items actually come to fruition — he bets ~75% won't (naming Razer's Project Linda as one he'd "almost bet money" never ships), while the rollable TV might arrive sooner than expected.
