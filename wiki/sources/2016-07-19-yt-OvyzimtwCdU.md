# The DROID DOES Throwback! (@mkbhd, 2016-07-19)

## Summary
A nostalgia/throwback video revisiting the 2009 Motorola Droid — **the first phone he ever reviewed** —
prompted by the "all phones look the same now" sentiment. Enormous biography value (origin of the review
channel), a restatement of the **"we measure phones by what's wrong with them"** doctrine, and a founding
statement of his "classic phones" interest. Ends with a question-to-the-audience format.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated)
- [2016-07-19] **BIOGRAPHY — the Droid was his first-ever review.** "This is my Droid — this is the first
  thing I ever reviewed. Should I link it? Sure, I'll embarrass myself. I used it every day for like two-
  plus years, and that's a long time for me." The literal origin of the review channel, stated on camera.
- [2016-07-19] **Restates "measure by what's wrong."** "I've seen comments saying phones are mostly
  similar now — big screens on rectangular bodies… and I said it before: we don't even really measure
  smartphones by how good they are anymore, we measure them by what's wrong with them, by how many flaws,
  and whether there are enough flaws to make a phone a dealbreaker." (Reinforces the pass-13 doctrine.)
- [2016-07-19] **The DROID DOES ad as a lost artifact.** "The Droid came out in 2009, same year as the
  iPhone 3GS — this phone was aimed directly at it; every rip in that commercial was a shot at the 3GS.
  You don't even see an entire commercial dedicated to comparing yourself to another phone like that
  anymore — I'm so glad we got it." A source-critical media-history read (physical keyboard, widgets,
  removable back/microSD, flash vs. the 3GS's none).
- [2016-07-19] **The megapixel-myth timestamp.** "If there was ever a time you could convince someone
  that higher megapixels automatically makes a camera better, it's 2009 (Droid's 5 MP vs. the 3GS's 3
  MP)." His anti-megapixel-myth stance, dated to its origin era.
- [2016-07-19] **Founds the "classic phones" interest + a question format.** "It was fun to look back at
  a device that made a big difference in how phones are talked about… I want to look at other classics —
  the first iPhone, the T-Mobile G1." Closes by asking the audience: "what's the oldest phone you could
  still use today? I think I could daily-drive this Galaxy Nexus pretty confidently." (Audience-engagement
  device + the seed of later retrospective content.)
