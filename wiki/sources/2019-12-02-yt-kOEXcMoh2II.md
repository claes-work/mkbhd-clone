# RETRO TECH: DYNATAC (@mkbhd, 2019-12-02)

## Summary
An episode of Marques's documentary series Retro Tech, tracing the Motorola DynaTAC 8000X — the first commercial handheld cell phone — from prototype to cultural icon. Marques narrates and hosts, unboxing an original DynaTAC (marveling at its heft, thickness, and 1G-only network), then interweaves expert/collector interviews (Brad, Michael, Lisa, Gerard) with an on-camera sit-down with the phone's inventor, Dr. Martin "Marty" Cooper of Motorola. The historical arc: before cell phones you were tethered to a landline or a pay phone; Motorola's early "mobile" phones were trunk-sized car phones; Cooper's team was pushed to build a true handheld in six weeks to beat Bell Laboratories, and Cooper placed the first handheld cellular call — to his rival Dr. Joel Angel at Bell Labs — while walking down 6th Avenue in New York. The DynaTAC hit the market in March 1984 at $3,995 (~$10,000+ inflation-adjusted), 1.75 lbs, 13 inches tall, 30-number storage, 10-hour charge for ~30 minutes of talk time. It stayed a rich person's status symbol until pop culture (Wall Street, Saved by the Bell's "Zack Morris phone") drove demand. Two guest-YouTuber segments follow: a "Dope or Nope" retro-phone game with Austin Evans (Motorola StarTAC Rainbow, Siemens Xelibri 6, Nokia 7280 "Lipstick Phone"), and a pager-code decoding bit with Sara Dietschy at a surviving NYC phone booth. Marques closes reflecting that this is the legacy he feels closest to, and thanking Cooper, Motorola, and the DynaTAC for the world of portable communication that made his own career possible.

## Key claims
- [2019-12-02] Marques's first cell phone was a little blue Samsung flip phone, around 2005 — roughly 20 years after the first cell phone, the DynaTAC 8000X, was released. (Marques)
- [2019-12-02] The DynaTAC is many times thicker than a modern smartphone — Marques stacks "five, seven, ten of today's smartphones" against it and calls the shrinkage a demonstration of Moore's Law. (Marques)
- [2019-12-02] The DynaTAC ran on the very first wireless network ever created, retroactively called 1G; 1G networks have not been operational for ~20 years. (Marques)
- [2019-12-02] Before cell phones, you could call from home before leaving; emergencies or a broken-down car meant using a pay phone — until the car phone arrived. (Lisa, interviewee)
- [2019-12-02] Motorola was developing car phones; the car phone was the first mobile phone, "mobile" only in that it could fit in a car trunk, and drew a lot of power. (Brad and Michael, interviewees)
- [2019-12-02] The engineering challenge was shrinking the trunk-sized equipment and roof antenna into a handheld unit; a Motorola team led by Dr. Martin Cooper pushed to build it in about six weeks, knowing Bell Laboratories was competing. (Brad, interviewee)
- [2019-12-02] Dr. Cooper's own account: at the beginning the phone was "a great curiosity" and few believed everyone would own one — "except we did." (Martin Cooper, interviewee — the inventor)
- [2019-12-02] Cooper made the first handheld cellular call impromptu while walking down 6th Avenue in New York, phoning his counterpart Dr. Joel Angel at Bell Laboratories to tell him he was calling from a real handheld cell phone; the line went silent. (Martin Cooper, interviewee)
- [2019-12-02] Cooper never imagined the technology advancing this far; today there are more portable phones in the world than people. (Martin Cooper, interviewee)
- [2019-12-02] The production DynaTAC 8000X was 1.75 pounds, 13 inches tall, stored 30 numbers, took 10 hours to charge for ~30 minutes of talk time, and cost $3,995 (over $10,000 adjusted for inflation); it officially hit the market in March 1984. (Marques, narration)
- [2019-12-02] After launch, cell phones took off but only in a limited way because a ~$4,000 handset was seen as a toy for the rich; pop culture — the film Wall Street and Saved by the Bell's Zack Morris phone — turned the DynaTAC into a status symbol people bought to show off. (Brad, interviewee)
- [2019-12-02] In the mid-1980s the cheaper pager was introduced: a belt-worn box that buzzed when someone called your pager number, making you immediately reachable without checking an answering machine at home. (Michael, interviewee)
- [2019-12-02] Pagers spread through hip-hop and entertainment (referenced via Biggie Smalls' "Warning") and spawned a numeric-code vernacular — an early precursor to texting, emoji, and bitmoji. (Gerard and Lisa, interviewees)
- [2019-12-02] There are only three phone booths left in New York City; Marques uses one at 90th Street and West End Ave. for the pager segment. (Marques, narration)
- [2019-12-02] Guest verdicts in the "Dope or Nope" segment: Motorola StarTAC Rainbow (1998), $1,000 — nope; Siemens Xelibri 6 (marketed to women) — nope; Nokia 7280 "Lipstick Phone" (2004) — Austin gives it a "certified dope," Marques a nope. (Marques and Austin Evans)
- [2019-12-02] Panel thesis on legacy: mobile phones are the most-used electronic device in the world, and you cannot have the current information age without the DynaTAC — "not just communication history — history." (Lisa, Michael, Brad, interviewees)
