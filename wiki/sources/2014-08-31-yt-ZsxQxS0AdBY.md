# The Truth About Beats by Dre! (@mkbhd, 2014-08-31)

## Summary
A solo MKBHD commentary essay breaking down what you are actually paying for when you buy Beats by Dre. Marques concedes upfront that Beats have a reputation among audiophiles for being overpriced and sounding worse than similarly priced headphones — and says that is largely true, but also not the point. His core argument is about **margins** (the gap between manufacturing cost and retail price) and **perceived value**: Beats spend comparatively little of that gap on sound engineering and a lot on best-in-class packaging, celebrity endorsement, and advertising, which is why they own the premium-headphone market. He frames Beats as pioneers of musician/celebrity audio endorsement (starting with Dr. Dre) and argues that paying for brand and image is a legitimate choice — a $300 pair isn't twice as good as a $150 pair, so the buyer should decide whether they're buying sound or buying the name. He contrasts Beats with the Audio-Technica M50 as the audiophile counter-example. Opens with a comedic self-dialogue bit (Marques voicing both himself and his "clone").

## Key claims
- [2014-08-31] Beats by Dre have a reputation in the audio-enthusiast community for being overpriced and sounding poor for their price bracket, and that reputation is basically accurate — but sound is not the point of the product.
- [2014-08-31] "Margins" are the gap between what it physically costs to make something and what it sells for; that gap funds profit plus other costs (R&D, advertising, packaging). Every device has margins.
- [2014-08-31] Example of margins on a phone: the Samsung Galaxy S5 costs roughly $250 in parts to build but retails around $600, with the gap covering Samsung's research, advertising, and profit.
- [2014-08-31] Audio-Technica's M50 margin goes mostly into research, testing, and some packaging — not much advertising — which is also why most people have never heard of Audio-Technica.
- [2014-08-31] Beats also spend on research and testing but go further on packaging and advertising; Marques calls Beats' packaging/unboxing experience the best in headphones, beating anything Audio-Technica has done.
- [2014-08-31] The bigger spend is advertising: everyone (including parents) has heard of Beats, but most non-audiophiles can't name another audio company — a direct result of Beats' endorsement and advertising spend.
- [2014-08-31] Beats pioneered the musician-endorsing-audio model the way athletes endorse shoes, starting with Dr. Dre — a legendary producer whose name lends implied credibility to the headphones.
- [2014-08-31] Beats leverage heavy celebrity association: custom pairs branded for artists (Justin Bieber, Lady Gaga, Nicki Minaj), musicians wearing/showing them in videos and live shows (Skrillex, will.i.am, David Guetta, Lil Wayne), and athletes wearing them in public (LeBron James, Kobe Bryant, Tom Brady, Serena Williams).
- [2014-08-31] None of that exposure is free — Beats pay for music-video product placement and give away custom pairs to celebrities so the product appears as a fashion statement on red carpets and at events.
- [2014-08-31] Beats own 64% of the premium (>$100) headphone market — proof the strategy works.
- [2014-08-31] "Perceived value" is the game Beats are winning: the more people believe a product is worth, the more they'll pay; endorsements, design, unboxing, and custom in-store displays let Beats charge $300 for headphones Marques bets cost under $150 (elsewhere "less than $150") to make.
- [2014-08-31] Beats aren't the only brand with inflated perceived value — Skullcandy comes to mind — but Beats' 64% share shows they're executing it best, and that's "not necessarily a bad thing."
- [2014-08-31] Consumer bottom line: a $300 pair is not twice as good as a $150 pair (probably isn't, especially when much of the price goes to marketing/image). If you value design, unboxing, name, and reputation, Beats are great and the M50 "suck"; if you only care about sound, frequency response, and accuracy, the M50 are great and Beats "suck." Pick a budget, then decide whether you're buying sound or not.
