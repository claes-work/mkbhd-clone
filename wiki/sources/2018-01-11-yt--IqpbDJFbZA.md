# Dope Tech of CES 2018! [Part 1] (@mkbhd, 2018-01-11)

## Summary
Part 1 of a two-part Dope Tech set filmed on the CES 2018 show floor in Las Vegas (it was raining) — Marques's first-day roundup of the coolest gadgets buried in the chaos of "tech everywhere, people everywhere." He leads with Razer Project Linda, a concept laptop shell that docks the Razer Phone as its brain and trackpad, then runs through the Sennheiser HD 820 (closed-back headphone using concave Gorilla Glass to seal off the open-back HD 800 sound), HTC's twin Vive announcements (the all-in-one Vive Pro and a wireless adapter that untethers you from the PC), Razer's RGB desktop speakers and subwoofer, a wall of new Audio-Technica headphones including the $2,000 open-back ATH-ADX5000, Yamaha's booth (a giant drone plus a self-balancing Motoroid motorcycle that stands on two wheels), and DJI's cheaper Osmo Mobile 2 gimbal. Throughout he flags the recurring CES pattern of dope-looking hardware with no price and no release date, and the hard truth that a noisy show floor makes premium audio impossible to judge. Ends by inviting viewers to send tips via Twitter/Reddit and teasing Part 2.

## Key claims
- [2018-01-11] Razer Project Linda is one of the most buzz-worthy, news-making things at CES 2018; it's a concept laptop that uses the Razer Phone as its brain and trackpad, so the shell needs no processor, RAM, or speakers of its own.
- [2018-01-11] The smartphone-into-laptop convertible idea isn't new and "has never really been wildly successful"; Razer hopes to do it best, leveraging the Razer Phone's existing power and great speakers.
- [2018-01-11] The only actual hardware in the Linda shell is an RGB keyboard, a battery, a webcam up top, and a couple of ports — a headphone jack, USB-C for charging, and a full-size USB-A for accessories like a mouse; the phone pops in and out with a single button, sits flush, color-matches the Razer Blade Stealth, and a front notch exposes the phone's fingerprint reader and power button.
- [2018-01-11] He thinks Linda's design is well thought out and interesting, but questions whether it's practical, and — as is common at CES — it has no price range or release date.
- [2018-01-11] His main worry about Linda: building an entire accessory around a smartphone that will likely be replaced in ~9–10 months raises open questions about whether a Razer Phone 2 would stay compatible and keep the same dimensions — something he'll keep an eye on.
- [2018-01-11] The Sennheiser HD 820 aims to take his custom HD 800 setup "to a new level" by isolating sound with concave Gorilla Glass over the back of the earcups — retaining open-back traits (big drivers, wide soundstage) while sealing you off from outside noise for people who don't want to hear it.
- [2018-01-11] He listened to the HD 820 and can confirm they sounded incredible; he wants to get them into the studio, and expects they'll be expensive — his guess is roughly $2,000–$3,000.
- [2018-01-11] HTC announced two important Vive things: the Vive Pro, an all-in-one headset with built-in headphones, a top strap, a higher-resolution display, and better tracking for those wanting the best VR (the original Vive stays on sale); and a wireless adapter, worn like a "bug antenna," that tracks just as well but lets you walk around completely untethered.
- [2018-01-11] The Vive wireless adapter is a "game-changer" because it frees you from tripping over cables; it runs off a battery in your pocket — a small 3,000mAh pack lasts a couple hours, and you can plug into something bigger for longer sessions — and works with both the old Vive and the new Vive Pro.
- [2018-01-11] Razer also showed a ridiculous-looking, "badass" pair of desktop speakers plus a subwoofer, again with RGB at the base; he can't judge their sound because the show floor is too noisy.
- [2018-01-11] Audio-Technica brought ~10 new pairs of headphones, including gaming headsets, open-back models, and wireless noise-cancelling headphones apparently aiming to rival the Bose QuietComfort series.
- [2018-01-11] He tried Audio-Technica's top-of-the-line ATH-ADX5000 ($2,000 open-back); they sound great, but being open-back on a super-noisy floor means he can't fully appreciate them yet — most of the lineup is going on sale soon if not already.
- [2018-01-11] CES had "tons and tons" of drones; an unexpected one at Yamaha's massive booth looked about the size of three people and, purely by eyeballing it, seemed capable of ~200 mph — though he stresses he knows essentially zero real information about it.
- [2018-01-11] The coolest thing in Yamaha's booth was a self-balancing motorcycle that didn't impress him until he realized its side wheels weren't touching the ground — the bike stands upright on its own two wheels, shifting its weight to stay balanced as someone pushes it around.
- [2018-01-11] DJI showed the Osmo Mobile 2, a much cheaper smartphone gimbal — roughly $50–$70 less than the original — that's super lightweight but still does the same stabilization, smartphone control, grip, and start/stop; it's actually going on sale and he expects a lot of people to pick it up.
- [2018-01-11] This is only day-one coverage; he's heading back to the floor for more and asks viewers to send tips via Twitter and the subreddit — with Part 2 to follow.
