# A Google Nexus Program: Explained! (@mkbhd, 2012-10-22)

## Summary
A proposal-explainer: what if Google ran a **"Nexus program"** — an Intel-Inside-style certification
letting **any manufacturer** ship a Nexus-certified device (stock Android, Google-pushed updates) if
it meets hardware and software requirements. The most valuable content is the clear, mechanism-level
explanation of **why Android updates are slow** — a fragmentation/incentive analysis — which he uses
to argue the program's software requirement is the hard part. Effectively predicts the certified-
stock-Android model Google later pursued.

## Key claims
- [2012-10-22] **The proposal — a certification stamp**: "a Nexus program that makes it possible for any
  manufacturer to make a Nexus-certified device would be pretty freaking cool — just like Intel stamps on
  Intel-certified computers, there could be a Nexus stamp of approval from Google. But not just any phone
  can be a Nexus; it would have to meet a certain set of requirements." Anticipates the certified-stock-
  Android direction (Android One / GMS-style) Google later took.
- [2012-10-22] **Why Android updates are slow — the mechanism, explained**: "the reason not every device
  has Android 4.1 right now is because the updates are not pushed by Google. You have a Motorola phone
  with a Motorola skin — every time a new Android update comes out, Motorola has to make their update work
  with the new version before they push it out, and then the carrier pushes it. Basically you're not
  getting your updates from Google if you don't have a Nexus." A clean, dated account of the skin+carrier
  update-fragmentation chain.
- [2012-10-22] **Even Verizon's Nexus isn't spared** — the incentive point: "strangely enough, even if
  you have a Verizon Galaxy Nexus, you're still waiting for Verizon to push the update because they like
  to put some Verizon software on it." The carrier-controls-the-software critique (cf. the 2011 carrier
  threads) applied to updates.
- [2012-10-22] **The software requirement is the sticking point**: "software requirements — it would have
  to run a pure stock version of Android provided by Google, which means all updates pushed from Google,
  not the manufacturer or carrier. That is the most difficult part of the program, because while we'd all
  love to see it happen, it's not exactly the most feasible." He identifies exactly where the incentives
  break the ideal.
- [2012-10-22] **Hardware requirements sketched as future-proofing**: no front hardware buttons (software
  buttons + larger screen), ≥8 GB storage, ≥1280×720 display — "basic stuff like that."
- [2012-10-22] **Frames it as a thought experiment, audience-programmed**: "it's just a thought I had… what
  manufacturer would you get your Nexus from?" — invites the audience to co-develop the idea.
- [2012-10-22] Context: previews Google's Oct 29 event ("the playground is open, as Google says"), where
  the multi-Nexus/LG-Nexus rumors would resolve.
