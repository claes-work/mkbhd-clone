# OnePlus One Review! (@mkbhd, 2014-05-20)

## Summary
The full review of the OnePlus One (following the earlier Impressions + Top-5-Features videos) — a
new phone from a brand-new manufacturer, and "one of the most polarizing phones I have ever seen."
The hook is price: near-flagship specs ($700-class) for **$300 off-contract**. Marques finds the
**hardware essentially flawless for the money** and locates the phone's whole risk in its software:
it runs **CyanogenMod 11S**, so "this phone is going to live or die on CyanogenMod" and whether the
updates keep coming like they would on a Nexus. A dated, canonical statement of his **value-tier**
and **control-over-software** doctrines, plus the flagship-killer framing.

## Key claims (paraphrase, dated)
- [2014-05-20] **The polarization is about price, and price is about who's watching.** In the tech
  community the phone is impossible to get (invite system, demand ≫ supply); in his college class
  nobody knows what it is. The tech community cares because "**we care about specs**," and $250–$300
  off-contract for near-flagship specs "is turning a lot of heads." (The geeks-value-specs framing.)
- [2014-05-20] **Specs are flagship-class:** 2.5 GHz quad-core Snapdragon 801, Adreno 330, 3 GB RAM,
  13 MP camera, 3100 mAh battery — "all very similar specs to something like the Galaxy S5."
- [2014-05-20] **Design is one of its best assets** — soft-touch back, metal frame ("no matter how
  you hold it you're touching metal somewhere"), and genuinely-considered button placement that keeps
  a 5.5" phone usable one-handed. Real stereo speakers ("instead of fake stereo like the iPhone or the
  Nexus 5").
- [2014-05-20] **He went looking for the hardware catch and couldn't find one** — "as far as I can
  tell this phone is pretty much flawless, a miracle piece of hardware for the price." The one hardware
  oddity: very low in-call earpiece volume (possibly software-fixable).
- [2014-05-20] **What differentiates it is the software, not the hardware** — it's a CyanogenMod 11S
  phone (Android 4.4 KitKat base), heavily customizable (on-screen vs capacitive buttons, theme engine,
  blurred lock screen, "Hey Snapdragon" voice wake). The **control-over-software** value again.
- [2014-05-20] **The camera pleasantly surprised him** — "where I was probably fully expecting to be
  let down by the price tag, but nope, gets a thumbs up." Aggressive HDR like the Oppo Find 7a; no OIS
  but an action mode that forces a faster shutter to freeze motion.
- [2014-05-20] **The one real con is CyanogenMod's bugs** — reproducible force-closes and, notably, a
  "**palpable delay**" on the home button specifically (other buttons are fast); he A/B'd it against the
  GPe HTC One M8 to confirm he wasn't imagining it. Good-and-bad: a custom OS should be perfect on its
  one phone, but bugs *can* be fixed with updates.
- [2014-05-20] **Verdict: a damn good deal if you trust CyanogenMod to maintain it like Google
  maintains a Nexus.** "If you're willing to take that risk in CyanogenMod… it might just be a damn
  good deal for the risk you're taking." He owns being a big fan of the hardware and doesn't mind the
  size ("it's a big phone but I have big hands").
