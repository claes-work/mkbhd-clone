# The Broken Galaxy Folds: Explained! (@mkbhd, 2019-04-18)

## Summary
Marques explains the wave of Samsung Galaxy Fold review-unit screen failures that broke across tech Twitter within roughly 48 hours of the first units shipping, including his own. He recounts peeling what looked like a standard pre-installed screen protector off the inner display — a habit he applies to every phone — only for the screen to go black and start flickering within seconds, because that "film" was actually a bonded protective layer that is effectively part of the plastic display structure. He catalogs four documented breaks and splits them into two categories: two units killed by reviewers peeling the top layer (his own, and Mark Gurman's), and two that failed through apparently normal use without anyone touching the film (Dieter Bohn's developed a bulge with something under the film; Steve Kovach's display went "insane"). Marques argues the second category is far more concerning, notes that the peel-warning text existed on carrier packaging but was absent from every review unit's wrapper, and roots the whole failure in the physics of foldables: a folding phone needs a soft plastic display instead of glass, and that softness is the culprit. His framing is sympathetic-but-skeptical — this is a brutal PR hit for foldables broadly (it made him newly wary of the outward-folding Huawei Mate X), first-gen hardware is effectively a "public beta test," reviewers exist to surface exactly this feedback, and he's still rooting for the category to eventually get good.

## Key claims
- [2019-04-18] Four Galaxy Fold review units broke within the first couple of days, "all around the same time for separate but very related reasons," and Marques's own unit was one of them.
- [2019-04-18] There are two distinct failure modes: (1) breaks caused by reviewers peeling off the top film (Marques's own unit, and Mark Gurman's), and (2) breaks that occurred without anyone messing with the film, through apparently regular use (Dieter Bohn's and Steve Kovach's units) — and the second category is "much more concerning."
- [2019-04-18] On his own unit, the inner-display "screen protector" looked like the typical pre-installed film (with a lip around the edge, similar to the Galaxy S10's), so he began peeling it from the bottom corner; it felt more firmly glued than a normal protector, and within about five seconds — after exposing a dime-sized area — the whole screen went black, with a stripe and flickering in the top-left corner.
- [2019-04-18] Samsung was "totally understanding" when he emailed them and replaced the phone; he is deliberately not peeling the replacement unit's film.
- [2019-04-18] Failure-mode detail per unit: Dieter Bohn never touched the film but the display developed a bulge (something got under the film) that killed it a day later with stuck pixels; Steve Kovach also didn't touch the film but his unit appeared not fully intact and "half the display is going completely insane"; Mark Gurman peeled the entire layer off (it survived the peel) but the screen then died over ~12 hours, going from half-black to two-thirds-black to fully busted; Marques's own broke decisively a few seconds into peeling.
- [2019-04-18] PSA: anyone who pre-ordered should not remove the protective film — "it might as well be basically part of the screen."
- [2019-04-18] A warning about peeling the layer appeared on T-Mobile / carrier-related packaging (photo from "Dez" at T-Mobile), but this text was a late addition and was NOT on any of the review units' packaging — Marques confirmed this across his own wrapper and by checking with Lou (unboxing video), Joanna Stern, and Mark ("Soldier Knows Best").
- [2019-04-18] Even if a user never peels the film, a film with a raised lip will eventually collect dust and may start to peel on its own (he cites Joanna Stern's unit already beginning to lift), and — more worryingly — the display can still be damaged through normal use without touching the film at all.
- [2019-04-18] Samsung's official statement (paraphrased by Marques): they received a few reports about the main display on provided samples and will inspect the units in person; separately, a few reviewers removed the top layer and caused damage; the main display's top protective layer is part of the display structure meant to guard against unintentional scratches, and removing it or adding adhesive may cause damage — Samsung says it will ensure this is clearly communicated to customers. Marques notes "we don't really learn much from it."
- [2019-04-18] Root cause framing: a folding phone requires a plastic display instead of glass, and that soft plastic is the culprit — he guesses it "scratches at a level three with deeper grooves at a level four" (referencing his own scratch-test convention).
- [2019-04-18] This is "brutal as far as PR goes" not just for Samsung but for folding phones in general — it made him "way more skeptical" about the Huawei Mate X, specifically concerned about that phone's display being on the outside.
- [2019-04-18] He was never really going to recommend most people buy the ~$2,000 Galaxy Fold, but he stays optimistic about the category: as a tech fan you root for crazy new ideas to succeed so they reach a second and third generation and improve.
- [2019-04-18] On the "Samsung should have waited" criticism: Samsung says it spent eight years developing the Fold, so this is the point they judged ready — but failures happen at all scales with all tech (he lists butterfly keyboards, wireless chargers, exploding phones).
- [2019-04-18] Samsung was not (as of recording) delaying the Fold and still planned to ship the first generation; Marques frames first-gen foldables as feeling like a "public beta test," and says surfacing this feedback is "our job as reviewers."
- [2019-04-18] Takeaway on the form factor: it's a delicate phone — a robot's 200,000 folds don't translate to a lifetime of human use, "there's a thing called user error," plus new variables like a hinge, moving parts, and no water resistance; this is "a pretty big hurdle" on the way to the category getting good.
- [2019-04-18] He'll keep using the (replacement) Fold as his daily driver toward a full review, and he's still "rooting for folding phones to eventually get good," imagining a future ~2023 video where he can say they're really good.
