# Retro Tech: Robots (@mkbhd, 2021-04-15)

## Summary
A Retro Tech (Season 2) documentary on the humanoid-robot-companion dream. Marques hosts, unboxing the 1984 **Omnibot** (a slow, R2-D2-inspired programmable toy robot he keeps as an on-camera sidekick), and traces our fascination from early animatronics (Elektro) and sci-fi (2001's HAL, and especially Star Wars' R2-D2/C-3PO as "a paradigm shift in how human beings understood what was possible with robots") through the 1980s toy wave. A "Dope or Nope" segment rates retro robots (Nintendo R.O.B., 2-XL trivia robot, Sony Aibo). A cautionary tale covers Nolan Bushnell's **Androbot B.O.B.**, whose faked CES beer-delivery demo (a human handed the robot the beer offstage) foreshadowed real disappointment. It builds to the thesis that the robot future already arrived — as the **Roomba** (the servant robot that "is the vacuum" rather than pushing one) and specialized/invisible automation — reinforced by the **Uncanny Valley**. A hands-on build with **Judner (UrAvgConsumer)** assembling a Robotix kit, and a visit to **Mark Parsons** at the Consortium for Research & Robotics (with "Wen," NYC's largest industrial robot), close it out. Professionally captioned with reliable speaker labels.

## Key claims
- [2021-04-15] Humans keep wanting humanoid, relatable robots; early real ones (the 7-ft Elektro) were limited by the tech of the time, while sci-fi robots could "be anything." (Griffin, Mark Parsons, interviewees)
- [2021-04-15] Star Wars (1977) "was a paradigm shift in how human beings understood what was possible with robots" — and gave robots distinct personalities (R2-D2 vs C-3PO), driving demand for personal robots and the 1980s toy wave (Verbot, Robotix). "Our vision exceeded our abilities to create humanoid robots who functioned." (Gethard, Griffin, interviewees)
- [2021-04-15] Dope or Nope verdicts (Marques + Omnibot): Nintendo **R.O.B.** — dope ("how can you go wrong" with an old-school NES); **2-XL** trivia robot — nope ("just a trivia game in the shape of a robot"); Sony **Aibo** robot dog — dope. (Marques)
- [2021-04-15] The Androbot cautionary tale: Nolan Bushnell's Androbot unveiled **B.O.B.** ("Brains On Board") at CES 1983 with a Jobs-style demo — B.O.B. fetched a beer "and everyone loses their mind ... the only thing is, there was a human backstage handing the beer to this robot." Buyers were "upset and angry" at the real capabilities, but Androbot "did open people's minds ... that you could actually build robots." (Griffin, Gethard, interviewees)
- [2021-04-15] Build segment (with Judner/UrAvgConsumer): assembling a '90s Robotix humanoid kit rated ages 7+ is comically hard for two adults — a running joke about "a strong second-grader" — and Marques's aside: building a human-shaped robot only makes sense if it's to "do something to make your life easier." (Marques & Judner)
- [2021-04-15] The robot future already arrived as the **servant/specialized robot**: from The Jetsons' Rosie to the **Roomba**, "the epitome of a servant robot" — "not invasive ... executes a task that we all hate," and "either you have a robot that pushes a vacuum around for you or the robot is the vacuum." Kids anthropomorphize it like a pet; parents prefer an "abstract shape" they "don't feel like I need to hold a funeral" over. (Parsons & commentators, interviewees)
- [2021-04-15] The **Uncanny Valley**: "we don't like it when they seem too robotic," but "the closer we get to having a super humanoid robot, the more clearly we see the distinctions of what makes it not human." The greatest thrust of robotics will be "very specific, more specialized ... invisible" automation rather than the humanoid robot. (Mark Parsons, interviewee)
- [2021-04-15] At the Consortium (with Mark Parsons): "Wen," NYC's largest industrial robot — 9 ft at rest, reaches 14 ft, 2,816 lbs, >10-ft reach, extremely fast/strong/precise — is being "hacked" from manufacturing into a creative art-making tool (it draws Marques's profile picture in dots). Marques compares it to the keyframe-programmed **camera robot in his own studio**, and both agree the domestic future is narrowing robots' jobs, not building humanoids. (Mark Parsons, interviewee; Marques for the studio-robot comparison)
- [2021-04-15] Marques's closing thesis: "we do have our robot future already. But humans gonna human, and we're gonna keep trying to push our way forward and imagine a future that's better and more advanced than it is today, and I don't think that should ever stop." (Marques)
