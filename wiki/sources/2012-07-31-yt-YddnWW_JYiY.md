# Google Nexus 7 Review! (@mkbhd, 2012-07-31)

## Summary
The full Nexus 7 review — and its standout is a **business-model analysis**: he explains that Google
sells the $200 tablet at roughly cost as a **loss leader for the Play Store**, spelling out the
razor-and-blades logic and the $25 credit as the hook. It's the incentive-structure lens (promoted at
synthesis pass 8) applied to *why a product is priced the way it is*. Also confirms the
form-factor-creates-a-market thesis after a month of use, and reports Project Butter as more pervasive
than first thought.

## Key claims
- [2012-07-31] **The loss-leader business model, spelled out — the landmark passage**: "Google spends
  about $200 to make it and then sells it for about $200, but they're trusting that you will spend enough
  time in the Google Play Store on this device to start giving Google more money and make it worth
  itself. They even give you a $25 credit… once you run out of that free credit you're going to start
  spending money on apps, books, movies, games, TV shows… that's what they're banking on, and that's why
  they made a Nexus tablet for the Google Play Store." Incentive-structure reasoning applied to hardware
  pricing.
- [2012-07-31] **The $200 iPod touch anchor for value**: "you know what cost $200? The 8 GB iPod touch.
  That gives you a good idea of the bang for buck you get." A concrete comparison to frame value.
- [2012-07-31] **Form factor creates a new market — confirmed after a month**: "up until now there was
  never a Nexus tablet… they decided to go with a 7-inch form factor and that sort of intrigued me, but I
  turned out to like it a lot." He reports the 7" size genuinely differs from a phone using his own
  before/after: after a month on the 4.8" Galaxy S3, the Nexus 7 feels tablet-sized; going back to the
  phone, the phone feels tiny.
- [2012-07-31] **The phone/tablet app "hybrid" argument**: because of the 7" size and resolution, "it
  handles phone-optimized apps just as well as tablet-optimized apps — kind of a hybrid… the best of both
  worlds," reframing the "Android lacks tablet apps" criticism as a non-issue on this form factor.
- [2012-07-31] **Project Butter reassessed as more pervasive with use**: "as I spend more time with it
  I've actually noticed it's more universal around the entire operating system than I first thought…
  it's even inside of apps too." A dated instance of a longer-use verdict revising a first-impression.
- [2012-07-31] **Honest gripes, specific**: the power and volume buttons are too close ("I found myself
  blindly reaching to turn the volume up… and I press the power button right next to it instead"); the
  home screen won't rotate to landscape ("goes back to feeling like a large phone"); Wi-Fi-only + 8 GB is
  "a bad combo if you're away from Wi-Fi all the time" → he recommends the 16 GB.
- [2012-07-31] **Plastic-back-as-advantage restated**: "that plastic back does help Wi-Fi reception, it's
  almost always full strength" — consistent with the TF300 argument ([[2012-05-01-yt-dB_xQ2x5Ngo]]).
- [2012-07-31] **Display judged relative to price, honestly**: "while it isn't a retina display or
  anything crazy — it's 200 bucks — it's more than 200 pixels per inch at 1280×720 and it looks damn
  impressive anyway." Grade-on-the-price-tier in practice.
