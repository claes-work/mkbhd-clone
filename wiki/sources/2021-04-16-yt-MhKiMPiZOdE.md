# Retro Tech: Hyperconnectivity (@mkbhd, 2021-04-16)

## Summary
A Retro Tech (Season 2) documentary on communication tech — the one category, the episode argues, where sci-fi's promises have largely been fulfilled. Marques hosts, unboxing a 1984 **Phone-Mate answering machine** (recording a cassette-tape "Brownlee family" outgoing message), then tracing the arc from the landline (tethered, no caller ID, "you literally just missed out") through answering-machine "freedom," 1980s–90s landline features (call waiting, 3-way, call forwarding), and the long-teased video phone. A "Dope or Nope" segment with comedian **Chuck Nice** tests retro video phones and a mouse-phone. A nostalgia segment with creator **Sara Dietschy** relives **AOL / AIM** (dial-up, buddy lists, away messages, "Chick22Sara" / "MKBSD"). It ends on the next frontier — brain-computer interfaces — with a **NextMind** demo reading Marques's visual cortex, and speculation about telepathic communication. Professionally captioned with reliable speaker labels.

## Key claims
- [2021-04-16] Every modern phone ships with voicemail, but in the '80s you "had to build that and buy that" — the answering machine was "pretty revolutionary." (Marques, narration)
- [2021-04-16] The landline was a game-changer (first conversations with people not in the room) but you were "tethered to that wall," and before caller ID a missed call meant "you literally just missed out on what could have been." The answering machine "represented freedom ... freedom from being tethered to your home phone." (Adesina, Stephanie, Brenda, interviewees)
- [2021-04-16] 1980s–90s landline features were "a huge deal": call waiting, three-way calling, call forwarding, plus aesthetic novelties (clear phones showing "the guts of the technology"). But sci-fi kept "tantalizing us with video communication" (AT&T's "You Will" campaign) before the tech was there. (Adesina & commentators, interviewees)
- [2021-04-16] Dope or Nope verdicts (with Chuck Nice): 1988 Mitsubishi Visual Telecom picture phone (stores 3 stills, not video) — **dope** ("even in 2021 I kind of still want one"); 1999 Hyper VideoPhone (color, bigger) — dope but "a little less dope" ("there's levels to this"); 1998 3D Tel-Mouse (a mouse-phone) — **nope** ("a clever workaround idea that will inevitably die when you can do both at once"). (Marques & Chuck Nice)
- [2021-04-16] The internet's adoption hurdle: early on you had to physically unplug your phone to connect, so "why would we want to ... disable our telephone line" — "the trouble that AOL had to overcome." AOL blanketed the US with CD-ROMs ("at one point ... 50% of all CDs made in the USA were AOL CD-ROMs") and became how most Americans first got online. (Adesina, Brenda, interviewees)
- [2021-04-16] AIM (with Sara Dietschy): AOL Instant Messenger was "the first time we could have instant communication via text" — buddy lists, the online "ding," away messages, and dial-up anxiety ("everyone had to be off the phones"). Running gag: Marques (who "never used AIM") is "the most boring 6th grader ever" with "How's the weather?" and a "BRB. Doing homework." away message that "lacks emotion." "AIM was a symbolic beginning of freedom of communication ... my own world, with my friends, no teachers, no adults." (Sara Dietschy, interviewee; Marques for his own lines)
- [2021-04-16] Communication is the sci-fi category "where we've actually pretty much achieved a majority of the things that we've seen" — cellular, two-way wrist watches, video calls all exist. The proposed next step: a **brain implant** ("blinking and saying 'Mom,' and there she is right in your head"). (Commentator, interviewee)
- [2021-04-16] NextMind demo: a wearable reads the **visual cortex** at the back of the head — "it's reading my brain through my skull and my skin and my hair" — translating neural activity into on-screen control (moving a paddle in Brick Breaker). The rep frames richer thought-transmission as "totally feasible ... a technical challenge of having the right sensors ... at the speed of thought," and "this is definitely going to happen." (NextMind representative, interviewee; Marques for the hands-on reactions)
- [2021-04-16] Marques's closing thesis: from phone calls to AIM to instant multi-person high-res video calls we've come far, and "the future of not even having to lift a finger or say a word and you just transmit your thoughts to another person ... that's pretty wild. That's definitely something to pay attention to." (Marques)
