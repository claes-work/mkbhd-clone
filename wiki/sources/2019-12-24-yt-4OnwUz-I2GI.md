# Smartphone Awards 2019! (@mkbhd, 2019-12-24)

## Summary
Marques Brownlee's flagship annual "Smartphone Awards" for 2019, hosted solo from a desk covered in the year's phones. He walks through his recurring award categories — best big phone, best compact phone, best camera, best battery, design, best budget phone, bust of the year, most improved, and MVP/Phone of the Year — naming a winner (with a physical trophy) plus runners-up and honorable mentions in each. Framing throughout: 2019 stratified smartphones from ~$300 to ~$2,300, high refresh rate (90/120Hz) began to matter, foldables arrived, and it became "really hard to buy a bad new phone." The MVP went to the OnePlus 7 Pro.

## Key claims
- [2019-12-24] Best Big Phone winner: ASUS ROG Phone 2 — praised for making the best use of the extra space: Snapdragon 855+ / 12GB RAM, a high-res 120Hz OLED with no notch, dual front-facing speakers, headphone jack, dual rear cameras, and a 6,000mAh battery; gaming features that also make a great media/overall phone. Runner-up: Samsung Galaxy Note 10+ (big battery, stylus, big-phone design; only real complaint is losing the headphone jack). (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] Best Compact Smartphone winner: Samsung Galaxy S10e — the less expensive S10 that made almost no sacrifices (dropped only the less-important telephoto; moved fingerprint reader to a fast side button that's easier to reach on a small phone; kept the headphone jack). Runner-up: iPhone 11 Pro — a legitimate flagship at smaller size/price, same cameras/specs/software as the 11 Pro Max, differing only in smaller battery and screen. (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] Best Smartphone Camera winner: iPhone 11 Pro / 11 Pro Max — his stated favorite category; wins for (1) multiple focal lengths (ultrawide + standard + telephoto) and (2) prowess in both photos and video on all three cameras, plus big year-over-year gains in image processing (new low-light/Night mode, better portrait mode); notes computational photography now leads and Apple is near the front. Runner-up: Google Pixel 4 — in some situations takes slightly better, contrastier/"more confident" photos, but loses on video, microphone, and lack of ultrawide. Honorable mentions: Huawei Mate 30 Pro (sharp, detailed, quad camera; didn't come to the US, unreviewed) and Samsung Galaxy Note 10+ (this year's blind smartphone camera test winner). (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] Best Smartphone Battery winner: ASUS ROG Phone 2 — 6,000mAh; ~1.5 days at 90Hz and easily 2 days at 60Hz; best battery of any phone he'd used in a long time (no wireless charging, decent fast charging). Runner-up: iPhone 11 (successor to the iPhone XR's already-great battery). Honorable mention: Oppo Reno Ace — fastest charging he'd ever seen, "Super VOOC 2.0" 65W, 0–100% in ~30 minutes. (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] Design Award winner: Samsung Galaxy Fold — the most interesting and potentially most important phone of the year; first-of-its-kind (the first units, including his, broke and never shipped; the ~$2,000 version 2 that did ship wasn't very popular) but the only category of phone offering a bigger screen in the same pocket footprint; he loves the concept and the risk. Runner-up: Samsung Galaxy Note 10 (the smaller Note — first Note whose whole screen you can reach one-handed, keeps the stylus, aura glow). (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] Best Budget Phone winner: Redmi K20 Pro — started at $299 with high-end specs, a big notchless display, pop-up camera, headphone jack, triple rear cameras, decent battery; framed as a spiritual successor to the Pocophone. Runner-up (near co-winner): Google Pixel 3a at $400 — same great camera as the Pixel 3 (last year's camera-award winner) plus the stock/fast-updating Google experience and headphone jack. Honorable mentions: OnePlus 7T (~$700, flagship performance / best bang-for-buck all year) and Motorola One Hyper ($400, late-year, unreviewed). (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] Bust of the Year: Google Pixel 4 — explicitly not the worst phone on the desk, but the biggest delta between expectations and reality: underwhelming "forehead" design, 90Hz issues, battery problems, weak Motion Sense, no ultrawide, no 4K60, poor microphone, and a ~$900 launch price competing with flagships it didn't match (camera still great). Runner-up: Royole FlexPai — a straight-up bad phone that "almost shouldn't have existed"; a rushed first folding phone that still felt like a prototype (bad battery, camera, OS, design, feel). (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] Most Improved winner: ASUS ROG Phone 2 (its third trophy of the night) — improved not just on specs (6,000mAh, 120Hz OLED, Snapdragon 855+) but because ASUS listened to feedback and now lets you choose at setup, for the phone's whole life, between the busy ZenUI and a much cleaner near-stock Android; camera is still the weak point. Runner-up: iPhone 11 Pro — new camera suite/lenses and the biggest battery gains in the lineup (dropping 3D Touch helped). (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] MVP / Smartphone of the Year: OnePlus 7 Pro — the most-liked and most-complete phone of the year (Snapdragon 855, lots of RAM, notchless high-res AMOLED via pop-up selfie camera, solid battery, very fast charging, great OnePlus software). Its 90Hz display is where it separated itself and "really proved that experience does matter," and at under $800 it undercut $1,000 flagships. Runner-up: Samsung Galaxy Fold. Honorable mentions: Pixel 3a and iPhone 11 Pro. Notes this award goes to the best combination of attributes, not necessarily the phone you should buy right now. (Marques)
- [2019-12-24] Overarching take: 2019 stratified smartphones from ~$300 to ~$2,300, making the differences more visible; high refresh rate (90/120Hz) is emerging as a dividing line he expects to spread in 2020; and it's now "really hard to buy a bad new phone." (Marques)
